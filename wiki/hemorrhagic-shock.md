# Hemorrhagic Shock

**Summary**: The most common form of shock in trauma, caused by loss of circulating blood volume; classified into four classes based on volume lost and associated physiologic changes.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Overview

Hemorrhagic shock is the dominant form of [[shock]] in trauma. An average 70-kg adult has approximately **5 liters of circulating blood volume**. Loss of blood depletes both oxygen-carrying capacity (RBCs) and circulating volume simultaneously — crystalloid fluids replace volume but cannot replace oxygen-carrying capacity. (source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

## Classification: Classes I–IV

| Parameter | Class I | Class II | Class III | Class IV |
|-----------|---------|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Blood loss (mL)** | < 750 | 750–1,500 | 1,500–2,000 | > 2,000 |
| **Blood loss (% volume)** | < 15% | 15–30% | 30–40% | > 40% |
| **Pulse rate** | ↔ | ↔/↑ | ↑ | ↑↑ |
| **Blood pressure** | ↔ | ↔ | ↔/↓ | ↓ |
| **Pulse pressure** | ↔ | ↓ | ↓ | ↓ |
| **Mental status** | Slightly anxious | Mildly anxious | Anxious, confused | Confused, lethargic |
| **Base excess** | 0 to –2 | –2 to –6 | –6 to –10 | < –10 |
| **Transfusion need** | Monitor | Possible | Yes | Massive |
| **Shock Index** | < 0.7 | ↑ (≥ 0.9) | > 1.0 | > 1.0 |

> ↔ = normal range, ↑ = increased, ↓ = decreased
>
> Values represent trends, not absolute thresholds — significant overlap exists between classes.

### Class I
Minimal clinical changes. Compensatory mechanisms restore intravascular ratio. Most patients require only maintenance fluids if hemorrhage stops.

### Class II — Compensated Shock
Blood pressure typically **remains normal** due to sympathetic activation. Key signs are tachycardia, tachypnea, and **narrowed pulse pressure**. Patient often anxious or frightened. Urine output drops to 20–30 mL/hr. May not require transfusion if bleeding stops.

> A normal BP in Class II does not mean the patient is not in shock. This is compensated shock.

### Class III — Decompensated Shock
Most patients can no longer compensate. Classic triad: tachycardia (>120–140 bpm), tachypnea (30–40 breaths/min), hypotension. Urine output falls to 5–15 mL/hr. Typically requires blood transfusion and surgical intervention.

### Class IV — Imminent Death
>40% blood loss. Profound tachycardia, tachypnea (>35 breaths/min), systolic BP ~60 mm Hg, confusion or lethargy. **Minutes to live without intervention.** Survival requires immediate hemorrhage control + massive transfusion (≥3 units PRBCs/hour or ≥10 units/24 hours).

## Pathophysiology

When hemorrhage occurs:
1. Adrenal glands release **epinephrine** → increased heart rate and contractility
2. Sympathetic nervous system releases **norepinephrine** → peripheral vasoconstriction
3. Vasoconstriction shunts blood from skin, bowel, and kidneys to heart and brain
4. Peripheral cells shift to **anaerobic metabolism** → lactic acid accumulates
5. ADH released from pituitary, aldosterone from adrenals → sodium and water retention (hours to take effect)

Compensatory mechanisms maintain vital signs until ~30% blood loss (Class III threshold), masking the severity. A falling BP is a **late** sign.

## Sources of Internal Hemorrhage

Blood can accumulate invisibly in:
- **Chest**: hemothorax — each pleural space can hold 2–3 L
- **Abdomen/pelvis**: intraperitoneal or retroperitoneal
- **Pelvis**: pelvic fractures can cause massive retroperitoneal hemorrhage
- **Long bones**: femur fracture ~500–1,000 mL; multiple fractures compound losses

An adult with 4 rib fractures + humerus + femur + bilateral tib/fib fractures can develop Class III/IV hemorrhage with no external blood loss.

## Coagulation and the Clotting Cascade

The clotting cascade has 13 identified factors, progressing through initiation → amplification → propagation to form fibrin. In massive hemorrhage:
- Clotting factors are diluted or consumed
- Hypothermia impairs enzyme function
- Acidosis further impairs clotting
- This creates [[lethal-triad]] — a self-reinforcing cycle

**Optimal replacement**: packed RBCs + fresh frozen plasma at 1:1 or 1:2 ratio. Platelets and cryoprecipitate added as needed. Whole blood is ideal when available.

## Permissive Hypotension

For **uncontrolled internal hemorrhage**, targeting SBP 80–90 mm Hg (MAP 60–65 mm Hg) avoids:
- Dislodging newly formed clots
- Diluting clotting factors with crystalloid
- Increasing bleeding pressure

**Exception**: TBI or spinal cord injury — maintain SBP ≥ 110 mm Hg to prevent secondary neurologic injury.
