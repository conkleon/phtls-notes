# MVCs and Blunt Trauma

**Summary**: Motor vehicle crashes, motorcycle crashes, pedestrian impacts, and falls — injury patterns predicted by crash type, occupant position, restraint use, and regional anatomy.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-16

---

## Overview

Blunt trauma involves two forces — **shear** and **compression** — that produce cavitation without penetrating the skin.

- **Compression**: an organ is directly squeezed between structures (e.g., spleen between steering wheel and vertebral column)
- **Shear**: two attached structures decelerate at different rates and tear apart (e.g., aortic arch continues moving while descending aorta is fixed to spine)

Motor vehicle crashes are the most common cause of serious blunt trauma. In 2019 in the United States, 36,096 people died and 2.74 million were injured in MVCs. (source: PHTLS 10th Ed., Chapter 4)

## The Five MVC Impact Types

### 1. Frontal Impact

The most common serious MVC type. Occupant takes one of two paths:

| Path | Mechanism | Injuries |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Up-and-over** | Head leads over steering wheel → windshield | TBI, facial fractures, bull's-eye windshield sign, cervical spine injury, sternal/rib fractures, aortic shear |
| **Down-and-under** | Body slides under steering wheel | Knee/dashboard impact → patellar fx, femoral fx, hip dislocation, posterior acetabular fracture |

**Aortic shear mechanism**: The arch and ascending aorta are mobile; the descending aorta is tethered to the spine. Rapid deceleration causes shearing at the isthmus. May produce immediate exsanguination or a contained traumatic aneurysm that ruptures hours to days later.

### 2. Lateral (Side) Impact

- Lateral compression fractures of ribs → pneumothorax, hemothorax
- Contralateral aortic shear (arch swings toward impact)
- Lateral cervical spine injury — head pivots toward impact, vertebral bodies separate on opposite side
- Pelvic fractures (open-book mechanism if impact over pubic symphysis)
- Spleen (left-side impact) or liver (right-side impact) compression injuries

### 3. Rear Impact

- Vehicle struck from behind → rapid forward acceleration of torso
- Head lags behind → hyperextension of neck → cervical ligament tears, whiplash
- Energy dissipation reduced if headrest is improperly positioned or absent
- Risk of second impact if vehicle is then pushed into an obstacle in front

### 4. Rotational Impact

- Vehicle pivots around point of impact
- Occupants experience combined frontal + lateral forces
- Produces compound injury patterns from both directions
- Unrestrained occupants may be partially ejected

### 5. Rollover

- Vehicle tumbles; multiple sequential impacts to occupants
- **Most unpredictable** injury pattern
- Unrestrained occupants may be completely ejected — ejection risk of death is **6× greater** than non-ejection
- ~25% of vehicular deaths involve ejection (source: PHTLS 10th Ed., Chapter 4)

## Restraint Systems

### Seat Belts

Properly worn seat belts reduce:
- Fatal injury risk by **45%** for front-seat occupants
- Serious injury risk by **50%**

In 2017 alone, seat belts saved an estimated 14,955 lives in the US. (source: PHTLS 10th Ed., Chapter 4)

Of 22,215 passenger vehicle occupants who died in 2019, **47% were unbelted**.

**Improper belt use injuries** (when belt is worn above the pelvis):
- Compression of abdominal viscera between belt and posterior wall → spleen, liver, pancreas laceration
- Diaphragmatic rupture from sudden intra-abdominal pressure increase
- Compression (Chance) fractures of T12-L2 as torso pivots over a fixed lap belt
- Small bowel "blowout" ruptures

### Airbags

- Designed to cushion frontal impacts (effective within 30° of headlights)
- Dissipate energy by increasing the body's stopping distance
- Deploy and **fully deflate within 0.5 seconds** — not protective in secondary or rear impacts
- **Minor injuries** from airbag deployment: facial/arm/chest abrasions, corneal injury, eyeglass-frame injury
- **Dangerous to children** in front seat — children 12 and under must be in rear seat; rear-facing seats must never be placed in front

## Motorcycle Crashes

Motorcyclists lack structural protection. Three impact subtypes:

### Head-On Impact
- Motorcycle stops; center of gravity above and behind front axle → tips forward
- Rider over handlebars → head/chest/abdomen/pelvis impact depending on trajectory
- Feet-on-pegs → thighs hit handlebars → **bilateral femur fractures**
- Pelvis into handlebars → open-book pelvic injury, pubic symphysis disruption, life-threatening intra-pelvic hemorrhage

### Angular Impact
- Motorcycle collapses on rider "like scissors"
- Rider trapped between motorcycle and object struck
- Lower extremity fractures, soft-tissue crush
- Abdominal compression injuries

### Ejection Impact
- No restraint → rider flies free
- Injury at point of impact (head, arms, chest, abdomen, legs against vehicle/pole/road)
- Energy radiates from impact point through body

**"Laying the bike down"**: protective maneuver — rider drags inside leg to separate from motorcycle before impact, accepts road rash and minor fractures to avoid catastrophic crushing. Works unless the rider directly strikes another object.

**Helmets**: reduce head injury and death risk significantly. Helmet structure (strong shell + energy-absorbing liner) mirrors skull anatomy.

## Pedestrian Injuries

### Adult Three-Phase Pattern

1. **Phase 1** — Bumper strikes lower legs: tibia/fibula fractures
2. **Phase 2** — Hood/windshield strikes torso: upper femur, pelvis, rib, spine fractures; intra-abdominal/thoracic injury
3. **Phase 3** — Fall to ground (usually head-first): head/cervical spine injury, unilateral hip/shoulder injury

**Large-fronted vehicles** (trucks, SUVs) hit the entire pedestrian simultaneously, bypassing the sequential phases.

### Children vs. Adults

| Feature | Adult | Child |
|---------|-------|-------|
| First contact point | Lower legs (bumper height) | Upper legs or pelvis (child is shorter) |
| Second contact | Hood/windshield at chest/abdomen | Front of hood at thorax; head on hood |
| Ejection | Usually thrown clear | May be dragged under vehicle |
| Head injury | Common | Universal — spine always suspect |

Children facing toward an approaching car (curiosity) receive frontal injuries; adults facing away (escape) receive posterior injuries.

## Falls

Key variables: height of fall, surface compressibility, and which body part struck first.

**Height thresholds for severe injury**:
- Adults: > 20 ft (6.1 m)
- Children: > 10 ft (3 m) — approximately 2–3× the child's own height

### Feet-First Fall Injury Sequence

Energy travels upward through the body:

1. Bilateral calcaneus fractures
2. Ankle fractures (tibia/fibula)
3. Tibial plateau fractures, femur fractures
4. Hip fractures
5. Thoracic/lumbar compression fractures (body weight from above still in motion)
6. Hyperflexion at concave curves of spine → compression fractures (concave side) + distraction fractures (convex side)

### Head-First Fall (Diving Injuries)
- Entire body weight compresses head and cervical spine
- Unstable cervical fractures — identical mechanism to up-and-over frontal MVC

### Outstretched-Arm Landing
- Wrist fractures (Colles' fracture pattern)

## Regional Effects of Blunt Trauma

### Head
- **Compression**: skull fracture; bone fragments driven into brain
- **Shear**: brain continues forward after skull stops → cerebral contusion, laceration, epidural/subdural hemorrhage, diffuse axonal injury; brain stem separation if severe

### Neck
- **Compression**: cervical spine angulation/compression → jumped facets, burst fractures, spinal cord injury
- **Shear**: lateral head pivot → ligament tears, rotational fractures

### Thorax
- **Compression**: rib fractures, flail chest, pulmonary contusion
- **"Paper bag effect"**: victim inhales and holds breath before impact; glottis closes; compressed chest wall causes lung rupture like a sealed paper bag
- **Cardiac contusion**: heart compressed between sternum and spine → dysrhythmias
- **Shear**: aortic tear at isthmus (mobile arch vs. tethered descending aorta)

### Abdomen
- **Compression**: solid organ rupture (spleen, liver, kidneys); sudden intra-abdominal pressure → diaphragm rupture → herniation, impaired ventilation, hemothorax
- **Shear (ligamentum teres)**: liver descends during down-and-under path; ligamentum teres (umbilicus to left lobe) acts as a cheese-cutting wire → transects liver
- **Shear (mesentery)**: kidneys, small/large intestine, spleen tear at points of mesenteric attachment

### Pelvis
- **Lateral compression**: femoral head driven into acetabulum; pelvic ring fractures in ≥2 locations
- **Anterior compression**: symphysis pubis disruption → open-book fracture → life-threatening intra-pelvic hemorrhage
- 4–15% of pelvic fractures associated with genitourinary injury

## Sports Injuries

Principles are identical to MVC kinematics:
- Assess forces, direction, deceleration, protective equipment
- Broken equipment (helmet, ski, stick) is evidence of significant force
- High-speed activities (skiing, snowmobiling, ATVs) can produce MVC-equivalent injuries
- Two colliding athletes: injuries in one are a guide to injuries in the other
