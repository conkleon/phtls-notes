# Kinematics

**Summary**: The physics framework underlying all traumatic injury — energy laws, kinetic energy, stopping distance, cavitation, and the three-collision model that explains how energy reaches internal organs.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-16

---

## What Is Kinematics?

Kinematics is the study of motion without reference to the forces that cause it. In trauma care, kinematics describes the energy exchange between moving objects and the human body. Understanding it allows prehospital practitioners to anticipate injuries that are not yet visible and raise the index of suspicion before examination begins.

> "Many of a patient's injuries can be predicted by a proper survey of the scene, even before examining the patient."
> (source: PHTLS 10th Ed., Chapter 4)

---

## Newton's Laws Applied to Trauma

| Law | Statement | Trauma Relevance |
|-----|-----------|-----------------|
| **First** (Law of Inertia) | A body in motion stays in motion unless acted on by an outside force | Unrestrained occupants continue moving after the vehicle stops |
| **Second** (Conservation of Energy) | Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only changed in form | The energy of a crash must go somewhere — vehicle deformation, tissue injury, or heat |
| **Third** (Equal & Opposite Reaction) | Every action has an equal and opposite reaction | Recoil of a firearm; force of impact transmitted back to the body |

## Kinetic Energy Formula

$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

Where **m** = mass (weight) and **v** = velocity (speed).

**Key insight**: velocity is exponential, mass is linear. Doubling speed quadruples energy; doubling mass only doubles it.

### Example

A 150 lb person at 30 mph: KE = 150/2 × 30² = **67,500 units**

| Change | New KE | Increase |
|--------|--------|----------|
| +10 lb (mass to 160 lb) | 72,000 | +4,500 |
| +10 mph (speed to 40 mph) | 120,000 | **+52,500** |

This is why high-speed crashes are so much more lethal than low-speed ones — and why slowing down saves far more lives than reducing vehicle weight.

## Stopping Distance

The shorter and more abrupt the stop, the more energy is transferred to the body. Longer stopping distance = less injury.

| Scenario | Stopping Distance | Injury |
|----------|-----------------|--------|
| Braking car | Long | Minimal — energy dissipated as brake heat |
| Seat-belted occupant in crash | Moderate | Energy shared between restraint and body |
| Unrestrained occupant in crash | Very short | All energy absorbed by soft tissue and bone |
| Fall onto concrete vs. snow | Short vs. long | Far greater injury on concrete |

## The Three Collisions

Every blunt trauma event — not just car crashes — involves three distinct collisions:

1. **Vehicle (or object) hits another object** — the visible crash
2. **Occupant hits the inside of the vehicle** — body collides with steering wheel, dashboard, windshield, seat belt
3. **Internal organs hit the body wall** — organs continue moving after the outer body stops; heart against sternum, brain against skull, kidneys tearing at their pedicles

The third collision is the one that kills. It is invisible from the outside and must be inferred from the mechanism.

> Note: In a fall, only the 1st and 3rd collisions apply (body→ground, then organs→body wall).

---

## Two Paths in Frontal Impact

When a vehicle strikes an object head-on, the unrestrained occupant takes one of two paths:

### Up-and-Over Path
- Head leads the body over the steering wheel
- Head/face strikes windshield → skull fracture, TBI, cervical spine injury
- Chest follows → sternum, ribs, cardiac contusion, aortic shear
- Recognizable by: bull's-eye windshield fracture, bent steering wheel

### Down-and-Under Path
- Occupant slides forward and downward under the steering wheel
- Knees impact dashboard → patellar fracture, femoral fracture, hip dislocation
- Abdomen may impact steering wheel → compression of solid organs
- Recognizable by: knee imprint in dashboard

## Cavitation

When a moving object strikes tissue, it pushes tissue particles away from the impact point. Those particles then strike adjacent particles, creating a "falling domino" effect that propagates outward. This creates a **cavity** — a hole in the tissue.

Two types:

| Type | Description | Visibility |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Temporary cavity** | Tissue stretches at moment of impact, then rebounds due to elasticity | Not visible at examination |
| **Permanent cavity** | Residual destruction after rebound; includes a crush zone from direct contact | Visible |

The ratio of temporary to permanent cavity depends on **tissue elasticity**:
- Elastic tissue (lung, muscle): large temporary cavity → small permanent cavity
- Inelastic tissue (liver, brain, bone): temporary ≈ permanent cavity

High-velocity projectiles (rifles) create massive temporary cavities — organs away from the bullet path can be severely injured by the shock wave alone.

---

## Tissue Density and Energy Exchange

Denser tissue absorbs more energy per unit volume. Three density levels in the body:

| Density | Examples | Energy Absorbed |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| Air | Lung, bowel | Low — compressible |
| Water | Muscle, solid organs (liver, spleen) | Moderate |
| Solid | Bone | High — rigid, shatters |

A bullet passing through lung transfers far less energy than one passing through liver.

## Blunt vs. Penetrating Trauma

| Feature | Blunt | Penetrating |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| Skin broken? | No | Yes |
| Cavity type | Temporary only (rebounds) | Temporary + permanent |
| Energy distribution | Over large area | Concentrated along path |
| Cavity direction | Away from impact point | Along missile path (+ lateral spread) |

Both involve cavitation; only the mechanism differs.

---

## Using Kinematics in Assessment

The scene survey should answer:
- What forces acted and from which direction?
- What objects were struck, and how much energy was involved?
- Was the patient restrained, helmeted, or protected?
- What part of the body absorbed the energy first?
- What organs lie in the path of that energy transfer?

This mental model is applied before touching the patient — it directs the physical examination and raises suspicion for occult injuries.
