# Aerobic and Anaerobic Metabolism

**Summary**: The cellular energy systems that sustain life; aerobic metabolism produces 38 ATP per glucose molecule using oxygen, while anaerobic metabolism produces only 2 ATP and generates lactic acid, driving metabolic acidosis in shock.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Aerobic Metabolism (Normal State)

The primary energy pathway for all human cells:

> Glucose + Oxygen → **38 ATP** + H₂O + CO₂

- Glucose is converted to pyruvate, which enters the **citric acid cycle** as acetyl-CoA
- Produces water and carbon dioxide as byproducts — both easily eliminated
- Requires a patent airway, adequate ventilation, functioning circulation, and sufficient RBCs

This is the target of prehospital trauma care: maintain aerobic metabolism in every cell. (source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

## Anaerobic Metabolism (Shock State)

When oxygen delivery fails, the citric acid cycle is disrupted:

> Glucose → **2 ATP** + **Lactic acid (lactate)**

- 19× less efficient than aerobic metabolism
- Lactic acid accumulation → **metabolic acidosis** (decreased blood pH)
- When pH < 7.20 → **severe myocardial depression**
- Cannot sustain cellular function indefinitely — cells die without reversal

Some organs (brain, heart, skeletal muscle) can use lactate as a temporary fuel source, but energy yield remains far below aerobic levels.

## The Energy Deficit Cascade

```
Oxygen delivery fails
        ↓
Anaerobic metabolism begins
        ↓
Lactic acid accumulates → acidosis
        ↓
pH < 7.20 → myocardial depression
        ↓
Reduced cardiac output → less oxygen delivery
        ↓
More anaerobic metabolism (self-reinforcing)
        ↓
Cell death → organ failure → patient death
```

## Organ Ischemic Sensitivity

Different organs tolerate oxygen deprivation for vastly different durations:

| Organ | Warm Ischemia Tolerance |
|-------|------------------------|
| Heart, brain, lungs | **4–6 minutes** |
| Kidneys, liver, GI tract | 45–90 minutes |
| Muscle, bone, skin | 4–6 hours |

> Brain and heart cells that die are not replaced. Irreversible damage in under 6 minutes makes early intervention critical.

The progression from cell death to organism death can take as little as 4–6 minutes (brain herniation) or as long as 2–3 weeks (delayed organ failure from inadequately resuscitated shock).

## Ischemia, Hypoxemia, and Hypoxia — Distinctions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Ischemia** | Insufficient blood flow to provide oxygenation (flow problem) |
| **Hypoxemia** | Low oxygen content in the blood (oxygenation problem) |
| **Hypoxia** | Low oxygen content in tissues (delivery problem) |

All three lead to anaerobic metabolism, but through different mechanisms. A patient with adequate SpO₂ can still be hypoxic at the tissue level if cardiac output is severely reduced.

## Clinical Implication: Lactic Acidosis as a Marker

Serum lactate is a measurable surrogate for anaerobic metabolism. Elevated lactate indicates cells are oxygen-deprived even when vital signs appear normal. In the prehospital setting, lactate is not routinely measured, but its physiologic effects drive the clinical signs of [[shock]].

The acidosis generated by anaerobic metabolism stimulates the respiratory center → **tachypnea is often the first visible sign of shock**, as the body attempts to blow off CO₂ and buffer the acidosis.

## Prehospital Treatment Goal

Every prehospital intervention for shock targets reversal of anaerobic metabolism:
- Maintain airway → ensure O₂ on-loading to RBCs
- Control hemorrhage → maintain circulating RBCs
- Limit crystalloid overload → prevent interstitial edema that impairs O₂ diffusion
- Prevent hypothermia → preserve enzyme function and clotting
