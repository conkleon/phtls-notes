# Penetrating Trauma

**Summary**: Physics of penetrating injury — kinetic energy tiers by weapon type, the roles of profile, tumble, and fragmentation in energy transfer, cavitation in projectile wounds, and shotgun wound classification.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-16

---

## Physics of Penetrating Trauma

The same kinetic energy formula that governs blunt trauma applies to penetrating wounds:

$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

When a projectile enters the body, it transfers its kinetic energy to tissue cells. These cells are accelerated away from the bullet's path, creating a cavity. This process — **cavitation** — is the primary mechanism of tissue destruction in penetrating trauma.

> "The energy of the bullet's motion is exchanged for the energy that crushes these cells and moves them away (cavitation) from the path of the bullet."
> (source: PHTLS 10th Ed., Chapter 4)

As with blunt trauma, **velocity is the dominant factor**. A bullet at 3,000 ft/sec from a rifle causes far more tissue destruction than one at 900 ft/sec from a handgun, even if their masses are similar.

---

## Energy Levels by Weapon Type

| Category | Muzzle Velocity | Examples | Cavitation |
|----------|----------------|----------|-----------|
| **Low energy** | < 1,000 ft/sec | Knives, hand-driven implements | Crush cavity only; tissue displaced laterally |
| **Medium energy** | ~1,000–2,000 ft/sec | Most handguns | Small-to-moderate temporary cavity; elastic tissues rebound |
| **High energy** | > 2,000 ft/sec | High-powered rifles, military assault weapons | Large temporary cavity; permanent destruction well beyond bullet path |

The distinction matters clinically: a handgun wound to the abdomen may injure only the organs directly in the bullet's path. A rifle wound may destroy tissue several centimeters away from the visible track.

## Factors That Increase Energy Transfer

Three properties of a projectile determine how much of its kinetic energy is deposited into tissue:

### Profile (Contact Surface Area)

The larger the frontal area of the projectile, the more tissue particles are struck per unit of travel, and the more energy is transferred. Profile can be increased by:
- **Deformation ("mushrooming")** — hollow-point bullets are designed to expand on impact, increasing cross-sectional area dramatically
- **Tumble** (see below)
- **Fragmentation** (see below)

A bullet that passes cleanly through without deforming transfers only a fraction of its total kinetic energy.

### Tumble

Most bullets are spin-stabilized in flight (rifling). On striking tissue, the nose decelerates rapidly while the heavier base continues forward, causing the bullet to **tip end-over-end**. The bullet then presents its full length (rather than just its nose) to the tissue — greatly increasing the contact area and energy transfer.

Asymmetric bullet geometry promotes tumble. The AK-74 rifle round, for example, was specifically documented to tumble in tissue and produce severe wound channels.

### Fragmentation

When a bullet breaks apart inside the body, each fragment becomes an independent projectile, each delivering its own kinetic energy to a different tissue path. Fragmentation:
- Multiplies the number of wound tracks
- Dramatically increases the total volume of tissue destroyed
- Makes wound tracking and surgical exploration much more complex

Bone struck by a projectile can also fragment, turning cortical bone shards into secondary missiles that injure adjacent structures.

## Cavitation in Penetrating Trauma

Unlike blunt trauma (which produces mainly a temporary cavity), penetrating trauma creates **both** a temporary and a permanent cavity simultaneously:

| Cavity | Cause | Clinical Significance |
|--------|-------|----------------------|
| **Permanent (crush) cavity** | Direct tissue destruction along bullet path | Always present; visible at surgery |
| **Temporary cavity** | Rapid radial expansion of tissue as energy is released | Larger with higher velocity; rebounds in elastic tissue (lung, muscle); does not rebound in inelastic tissue (liver, brain) |

For high-energy rifle wounds, the temporary cavity can be **30–40× the diameter** of the bullet. Organs at a distance from the bullet track — but within the expansion zone — can be severely contused or lacerated without any direct contact with the projectile.

---

## Entrance and Exit Wounds

- The path of penetrating trauma is defined by the **entry wound** and, if present, the **exit wound**
- Exit wounds are typically larger (tumble, expanding profile at exit)
- Absence of exit wound means the projectile is retained — all kinetic energy was deposited in the body
- With retained fragments, bone or organ damage may be distributed in unexpected locations

---

## Regional Effects of Penetrating Trauma

### Head
- Skull is a rigid, closed container
- Bullet energy → immediate pressure wave throughout brain → diffuse injury beyond the direct wound track
- Pressure wave follows the curvature of the skull
- Retained energy has no exit → maximal destruction

### Thorax
- Lung tissue (air density): high elasticity, rebound → smaller permanent cavity despite temporary cavity
- Heart: water-density, inelastic → devastating cavitation; even handgun wounds are often fatal
- Aorta: immediate exsanguination from any penetrating wound

### Abdomen
- Solid organs (liver, spleen, kidneys): inelastic → large permanent cavity for any high-energy round
- Hollow viscus (bowel): may not be immediately life-threatening but causes peritonitis if delayed
- Diaphragm: any penetrating wound below the nipple line may traverse the diaphragm in both directions; thoracoabdominal injuries common

## Shotgun Wounds

Shotguns are unique because they fire **multiple projectiles** (pellets) in a spread pattern from a smooth-bore barrel. They use chokes/diverters to shape this spread. Because pellets are spherical and aerodynamically inefficient, they decelerate rapidly — making the **range from muzzle to target** the single most important variable in wound severity.

### Ammunition Components
- **Gunpowder** → propellant
- **Wadding** (lubricated paper, fiber, or plastic) → separates shot from powder; can enter wounds and become infectious
- **Shot**: birdshot (200–2,000 pellets), buckshot (6–20 pellets), slugs (single projectile)
- **Stippling**: gunpowder particles embedded in skin at close range ("tattooing")

### Shotgun Wound Categories

| Category | Range | Wound Appearance | Mortality |
|----------|-------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Contact** | Muzzle touching skin | Circular or stellate entry, searing/burning, widespread deep tissue destruction | 85–90% |
| **Close range** | < 6 ft (1.8 m) | Circular entry, soot/stippling present, wadding imprint adjacent to wound | 15–20% |
| **Intermediate range** | 6–18 ft (1.8–5.5 m) | Central entry wound + satellite pellet holes; mixture of deep + superficial injury | 0–5% |
| **Long range** | > 18 ft (5.5 m) | Classic scattered pellet spread; superficial but numerous wounds | 0% |

**Contact wounds** to the chest → open pneumothorax; to the abdomen → bowel evisceration. The standard shotgun barrel length makes self-inflicted contact head wounds difficult (arm reach problem) — attempts typically produce facial injury without brain penetration.

Even at **long range**, single pellets can penetrate deeply enough to:
- Perforate bowel → peritonitis
- Damage a major artery → vascular compromise to an extremity

Always examine **completely exposed** patients with shotgun injuries. A single circular wound could represent a contact/close-range birdshot injury (tight column, deep penetration) or a slug — both require the same high index of suspicion.

### Assessment Priorities for Shotgun Wounds
1. Determine range (scene information, wound characteristics)
2. Identify all entrance wounds — patient must be fully exposed and log-rolled
3. Gather scene information (shell type, number of shots, estimated range) to relay to receiving facility
4. Maintain high suspicion for internal injury regardless of wound appearance

---

## Using Penetrating Trauma Knowledge in Assessment

The prehospital practitioner should identify:
- **Weapon type** → energy tier
- **Range** (for shotguns and explosives)
- **Entry and exit wounds** → trajectory through body; which organs are in the path
- **Fragmentation** signs → multiple wound tracks, unusual bone injury patterns
- **Retained projectile** → all energy deposited in body

A wound that appears minor (small entry, no exit) from a high-powered rifle round may have caused massive internal destruction through temporary cavitation — the visible wound is a poor indicator of severity for high-velocity weapons.
