# Shock Assessment

**Summary**: Systematic evaluation for shock using XABCDE and MARCH frameworks, with emphasis on early signs (tachypnea, tachycardia, skin changes) over late signs (hypotension), and awareness of confounding factors that can mask severity.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Primary Survey Frameworks

### XABCDE (Standard Trauma EMS)

| Step | Focus |
|------|-------|
| **X** — eXsanguinating hemorrhage | Control life-threatening external bleeding immediately |
| **A** — Airway | Patency with c-spine control as appropriate |
| **B** — Breathing | Ventilation and oxygenation |
| **C** — Circulation | Perfusion and additional hemorrhage |
| **D** — Disability | Neurologic status / altered LOC |
| **E** — Expose/Environment | Identify hidden bleeding, prevent hypothermia |

### MARCH (Tactical/Military EMS)

| Step | Focus |
|------|-------|
| **M** — Massive hemorrhage | Tourniquet, pelvic binder, hemostatic dressing |
| **A** — Airway | Obstruction → position, NPA, advanced airway |
| **R** — Respirations | Chest wounds, tension pneumothorax |
| **C** — Circulation | Shock assessment, IV/IO access, fluid |
| **H** — Head/Hypothermia | TBI prevention, temperature preservation |

Both frameworks prioritize hemorrhage control before airway — a reversal of the traditional ABC sequence. (source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

## Primary Survey: What to Assess

### Exsanguinating Hemorrhage
- **Assess first** — a patient can bleed to death in minutes from major arterial injury
- Check for: scalp lacerations, wounds over major vessels (subclavian, axillary, brachial, radial, ulnar, carotid, femoral, popliteal)
- If no external hemorrhage → assume internal until proven otherwise

### Airway
Patients requiring immediate airway management (in order of priority):
1. Not breathing
2. Obvious airway compromise
3. Noisy ventilation (stridor, gurgling)
4. Markedly abnormal ventilatory rate

### Breathing — Earliest Sign of Shock
Anaerobic metabolism → lactic acid → buffer system → ↑ CO₂ → respiratory center stimulated → **tachypnea**.

> **Tachypnea is frequently the earliest sign of shock** — earlier than tachycardia.

- In primary survey: estimate ventilation as slow / normal / fast / very fast — do not count precisely
- **Slow + shock** = profound shock, possible cardiac arrest imminent
- SpO₂ < 94% → concerning, identify cause
- ETCO₂ unreliable in shock patients for guiding ventilation (poor correlation with PaCO₂ when perfusion is compromised)
- Patient removing oxygen mask + anxiety + confusion = **cerebral ischemia** ("air hunger")

### Circulation
Assess:
1. **Hemorrhage** — amount and source
2. **Perfusion** — total body and regional

Loss of radial pulse = severe hypovolemia. A "weak" radial pulse is associated with BP ~26 mm Hg lower than a "normal" pulse, and **15× higher mortality**.

### Disability (LOC)
Altered mental status = impaired cerebral oxygenation until proven otherwise.

Six causes of altered LOC in trauma:
1. Hypoxemia
2. Stroke
3. Shock (impaired cerebral perfusion)
4. TBI
5. Intoxication (alcohol, drugs, poisons)
6. Metabolic (diabetes, seizures, eclampsia)

> **Treat hypoxemia first** — it is the most treatable and the fastest killer.

Anxiety and belligerence are typically the **first signs** of cerebral hypoperfusion.

## Secondary Survey: Vital Signs in Detail

### Ventilatory Rate (Normal: 12–20 breaths/min)
| Rate | Interpretation |
|------|---------------|
| 20–30 breaths/min | Borderline — onset of shock, give supplemental O₂ |
| > 30 breaths/min | Late shock — assisted ventilation needed |

### Pulse Rate (Normal: 60–100 beats/min)
| Rate | Interpretation |
|------|---------------|
| 100–120 bpm | Early shock |
| > 120 bpm | Definite shock (unless pain/fear) |
| > 140 bpm | Critical |

### Blood Pressure — A Late and Unreliable Early Sign
- **Does not drop until ≥ 30% blood loss** (Class III)
- A dropping BP = patient can no longer compensate = **late, dangerous sign**
- Systolic BP is a poor early indicator — pulse rate, ventilatory rate, skin signs, and SI are more sensitive
- Brain injuries do not cause hypotension until herniation — hypotension + head injury = assume hemorrhage from other injuries
- **Exception**: infants < 6 months may bleed enough intracranially to cause hypovolemic shock (open sutures)

### MAP
MAP = DBP + ⅓ (SBP − DBP). Normal 70–100 mm Hg.
- Target MAP > 65 for neurogenic hypotension
- Target MAP ≥ 85–90 for spinal cord injury

### Shock Index (SI = HR ÷ SBP)
- < 0.7 = normal
- ≥ 0.9 = higher mortality, critical bleeding risk
- > 1.0 = Class III hemorrhage or worse

### Skin Color
- **Pale/mottled**: inadequate oxygenation to periphery
- Causes: peripheral vasoconstriction (hypovolemia), acute anemia, vascular interruption
- In **dark-pigmented patients**: assess lips, gums, nailbeds, palms

### Skin Temperature
- Cool skin = vasoconstriction, decreased cutaneous perfusion, anaerobic metabolism = **shock**
- Warm, dry skin = neurogenic hypotension (see [[neurogenic-hypotension]])

### Capillary Refill Time
- Test of **perfusion of the capillary bed**, not a test of shock per se
- Useful in combination with other signs
- Body shuts down most distal circulation first → nail bed of thumb/big toe provides early warning
- Improving capillary refill = resuscitation is working
- Confounders: cold environment, fractures, hypothermia, arteriosclerosis

## Confounding Factors

### Age
- **Elderly**: diminished compensatory ability — minor injury can cause decompensated shock
- **Children/young adults**: powerful compensators; may appear normal then suddenly decompensate; decompensated shock in a child = **dire emergency**

### Athletic Status
- Athletes have resting HR 40–50 bpm — tachycardia of 100 may indicate massive hemorrhage
- A HR of 50 in an athlete may be completely normal

### Pregnancy
- Blood volume increases 45–50% in pregnancy; CO and HR elevated
- May not show shock signs until > 30–35% blood loss
- Fetus adversely affected **before** mother shows signs (placental circulation highly sensitive to catecholamines)
- Third trimester: gravid uterus compresses IVC → elevate right side (left uterine displacement) to restore preload

### Preexisting Medical Conditions
- CAD, CHF, COPD: reduced compensatory ability
- Fixed-rate pacemakers: **unable to develop compensatory tachycardia**
- Diabetes: less compliant vessels, decreased hemodynamic sensitivity

### Medications
- **Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers**: prevent compensatory tachycardia — patient may stay bradycardic while losing blood
- **NSAIDs**: impair platelet activity → increased hemorrhage
- **Anticoagulants** (warfarin, DOACs): may prevent clotting for days; alter trauma center destination choice

### Time Between Injury and Treatment
- Rapid EMS response → patient may appear well with aortic or IVC injury (not enough time elapsed)
- **"Looks good" = compensated shock** in many cases
- Continued reassessment is essential — internal hemorrhage can present late

## Internal Hemorrhage: When to Suspect

When no external hemorrhage found, suspect internal sources:
- **Chest**: decreased breath sounds, mechanism of blunt thoracic trauma → hemothorax
- **Abdomen/pelvis**: ecchymosis, distension, tenderness, pelvic instability, leg-length inequality, perineal bruising, blood at urethral meatus
- **eFAST exam**: prehospital ultrasound to identify intraperitoneal fluid or pneumothorax (where available)

Non-hemorrhagic shock differentials when hemorrhage excluded:
- **Cardiac tamponade**: distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds, blunt/penetrating chest trauma
- **Tension pneumothorax**: decreased breath sounds unilaterally, subcutaneous emphysema, respiratory distress, tracheal deviation (late finding), distended neck veins
- **Neurogenic hypotension**: bradycardia, warm extremities, signs of spinal trauma

## Compensated vs. Decompensated Hypovolemic Shock: Quick Reference

| Vital Sign | Compensated | Decompensated |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Pulse | Tachycardia | Marked tachycardia → bradycardia (preterminal) |
| Skin | Pale, cool, moist | Pale, cold, waxy |
| Blood pressure | **Normal** | Decreased |
| LOC | Unaltered | Disoriented → coma |
