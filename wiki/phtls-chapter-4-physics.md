# PHTLS Chapter 4 — The Physics of Trauma

**Summary**: Source summary for PHTLS 10th Edition Chapter 4 — kinematics framework, blunt and penetrating trauma physics, MVCs, motorcycle and pedestrian injuries, shotgun wounds, blast injuries, and application to patient assessment.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-16

---

## Chapter Overview

**Chapter 4: The Physics of Trauma**
Lead Editors: Andrew Schmidt MD, Kelsey Wise MD, Brandon Kelly MD
Book pages: 103–147 (PDF pages 130–174)

This chapter bridges physics and clinical medicine. Its thesis: an understanding of energy exchange allows prehospital practitioners to **predict injuries before they are found** — or even before the patient is examined.

> "Knowing where to look and how to assess for injuries is just as important as knowing what to do after finding injuries."
> (source: PHTLS 10th Ed., Chapter 4)

---

## Opening Scenario

A single-vehicle crash before dawn. The vehicle struck a tree (front-end damage), spun around it, and reversed into a drainage ditch (rear damage). The restrained, airbag-deployed driver is moaning, slurred speech, smells of alcohol.

**Physics applied**: Multiple sequential impacts (frontal + rotational + rear). High index of suspicion for head/neck/chest/abdominal injury — confirmed on exam as abrasions over left clavicle, tenderness in anterior chest, mid-abdomen, and both lower abdominal quadrants.

## Chapter Objectives (Summary)

- Define kinetic energy and its relationship to injury
- Explain the association between laws of motion and trauma
- Explain the relationship between speed and energy
- Discuss cavitation and energy exchange
- Given an MVC description, predict injury patterns for an unrestrained occupant
- Describe injuries from interior and exterior vehicle damage
- Explain seat belt and airbag function
- Apply Newton's laws to non-MVC mechanisms (blast, falls)
- Define the five phases of blast injury
- Explain low/medium/high-energy weapon differences
- Discuss frontal surface area and energy exchange
- Integrate physics into trauma patient assessment

## Key Quotes

> "The physics of trauma deals with the motion of objects without reference to the forces that cause the motion. Any injury that results from a force applied to the body is related directly to the interaction between the host and a moving object that impacts the host."

> "Integrating the principles of the physics of trauma into the assessment of the trauma patient is key to discovering the potential for severe or life-threatening injuries."

> "Most injuries can be anticipated by understanding the energy exchange that occurs with the human body at the time of a collision."

## Chapter Summary (from text)

**Blunt Trauma**
- Impact direction determines injury pattern: frontal, lateral, rear, rotational, rollover, angular
- Ejection greatly increases injury severity
- Energy-absorbing systems (seat belts, airbags, energy-absorbing auto parts) are critical
- Vehicle damage indicates which occupants are most severely at risk

**Falls**
- Distance traveled affects severity
- Surface compressibility affects severity
- First body part to impact and subsequent energy transfer pathway determine injury pattern

**Penetrating Trauma**
- Low / medium / high energy: knives / handguns / rifles
- Distance from perpetrator affects energy at impact
- Organs in proximity to path determine life-threatening risk
- Entry and exit wounds define trajectory

**Blasts**
- Primary: blast shock wave
- Secondary: projectiles (most common)
- Tertiary: body propulsion
- Quaternary: heat and flames
- Quinary: radiation, chemicals, bacteria
