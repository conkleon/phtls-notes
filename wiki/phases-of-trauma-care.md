# Phases of Trauma Care

**Summary**: The three-phase framework for understanding and preventing traumatic injury — pre-event, event, and post-event — and the role of prehospital care practitioners in each phase.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Core Principle

> "Trauma is no accident, even though it is often referred to as such."

Most trauma deaths fit the definition of a **preventable event** caused by carelessness, not a true chance event. Traumatic incidents fall into two categories:
- **Intentional** — acts carried out with the goal of harming or killing
- **Unintentional** — events not intended to cause harm but resulting in injury

All three phases of trauma care offer opportunities for intervention.

---

## Phase 1: Pre-Event

**Definition**: Circumstances leading up to the injury.

**Focus**: Injury prevention — stopping the injury before it occurs.

**Analogy** from PHTLS: A community repeatedly sees cars slide off a winding mountain road and plummet 100 feet below. Stationing an ambulance at the bottom treats the result. Installing guardrails along the curve is the better alternative — it prevents the incident.

### Examples of Pre-Event Interventions

| Target | Intervention |
|--------|-------------|
| Motor vehicle crashes | Seat belt laws, speed limits, distracted driving campaigns ("It Can Wait," "U Drive. U Text. U Pay"), drunk driving enforcement |
| Falls | Fall risk screening in elderly, home safety programs |
| Drowning | Pool fencing, swim education |
| Firearms | Safe storage laws, trigger locks |

### Why Prevention Matters

- ~85% of Americans owned smartphones in 2021 (vs. 35% in 2011) → distracted driving deaths rose proportionally
- CDC estimates ~3,000 deaths/year from distracted driving
- Falls and MVC are the only two traumatic causes predicted to **increase** worldwide by 2030 (source: 2014 global report)

The prehospital care community has a professional obligation to advocate for injury prevention, not only to treat injuries after the fact.

---

## Phase 2: Event

**Definition**: The injury itself — the moment of impact, fall, or violent act.

**Focus**: Factors that influence severity of injury at the moment it occurs.

Prehospital practitioners can influence outcomes in the event phase through:
- Recognizing mechanisms of injury and injury patterns (see [[golden-principles]] — Principle 5)
- Understanding physics of trauma (kinetic energy, deceleration, penetrating vs. blunt)
- Pre-positioning of EMS resources in high-risk areas

---

## Phase 3: Post-Event

**Definition**: The period following the injury — from EMS activation through definitive care and rehabilitation.

**Focus**: Rapid, appropriate response; hemorrhage control; transport to the right facility; in-hospital care.

### Timodal Distribution of Trauma Deaths

Deaths following injury cluster in three time periods (each representing a different intervention opportunity):

| Timing | Cause | Preventable by |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| **Immediate** (seconds to minutes) | Severe CNS injury, aortic rupture, airway obstruction | Injury prevention (pre-event); rarely survivable even with ideal care |
| **Early** (minutes to hours) | Hemorrhage, head injury, tension pneumothorax — the **"Golden Period"** window | Prehospital care + rapid transport + early surgical hemorrhage control |
| **Late** (days to weeks) | MODS, sepsis, ARDS, complications | ICU care, damage control surgery, infection control |

The **early** deaths are where prehospital care has its greatest impact. This is the rationale for the [[golden-period]] concept and the Platinum 10 Minutes rule.

### Post-Event Prehospital Responsibilities

1. Gain access to the patient
2. Identify and treat life-threatening injuries
3. Minimize scene time — perform only interventions that reverse immediately life-threatening conditions
4. Transport to the closest **appropriate** facility by the most expeditious mode

---

## The EMS Practitioner's Role Across All Three Phases

| Phase | Role |
|-------|------|
| Pre-event | Injury prevention education, community advocacy |
| Event | Positioning, rapid response, recognition of mechanism |
| Post-event | Primary survey, hemorrhage control, airway, transport, en-route treatment |
