# The Golden Period

**Summary**: The concept — originally described as the "Golden Hour" by R. Adams Cowley, MD — that critically injured trauma patients have a finite window within which definitive care must be initiated to maximize survival. Includes the "Platinum 10 Minutes" rule for scene time.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Origin

R. Adams Cowley, MD, founder of the Maryland Institute of Emergency Medical Services Systems (MIEMSS) — one of the first trauma centers in the United States — described the concept in an interview:

> "There is a 'golden hour' between life and death. If you are critically injured, you have less than 60 minutes to survive. You might not die right then—it may be three days or two weeks later—but something has happened in your body that is irreparable."

Dr. Cowley was describing a **concept**, not a literal 60-minute clock.

---

## Why "Golden Period" Is More Accurate Than "Golden Hour"

- The time window varies by patient and injury
- A patient with a penetrating wound to the heart may have **only minutes** before shock becomes irreversible
- A patient with slow ongoing internal hemorrhage from an isolated femur fracture may have **several hours**
- The term "Golden Period" better captures the variable, patient-specific nature of this window

The American College of Surgeons Committee on Trauma uses this concept to emphasize the importance of transporting trauma patients to expert care **in a timely manner** — not as a rigid 60-minute rule.

---

## Why Trauma Patients Die: Trimodal Distribution

| Phase | Timing | Predominant Cause | Intervention |
|-------|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Immediate** | Seconds to minutes | Severe CNS injury, aortic rupture, massive hemorrhage | Largely unpreventable; injury prevention is the only lever |
| **Early** | Minutes to hours | Hemorrhagic shock, expanding hematomas, tension pneumothorax, airway obstruction | **Prehospital care and rapid transport — the Golden Period window** |
| **Late** | Days to weeks | MODS, sepsis, ARDS, pulmonary embolism | ICU-level care, damage control surgery |

The majority of **preventable** trauma deaths occur in the **early phase**. This is where prehospital care has its greatest impact.

## Implications for Scene Time

Every minute spent on scene is a minute during which:
- The patient continues to bleed
- Valuable time is consumed from the Golden Period

### Real-World Time Analysis (US Urban EMS)

| Interval | Time |
|----------|------|
| Injury to 9-1-1 call | Variable |
| EMS activation to arrival on scene | ~8–9 min |
| Scene time | 10+ min (goal: ≤10 min for critical patients) |
| Transport to receiving facility | ~8–9 min |
| **Total time before hospital arrival** | >30 min at minimum |

A 1980s–1990s study at one trauma center documented scene times of **20–30 minutes** for MVC and penetrating trauma — and this was associated with higher mortality.

One study showed critically injured patients transported by **private vehicle** had lower mortality (17.9% vs. 28.2%) than those transported by ambulance — likely because EMS spent too long on scene.

### The Key Questions Every Practitioner Must Ask

1. Is what I am doing going to benefit this patient?
2. Does that benefit outweigh the risk of delaying transport?

## The "Platinum 10 Minutes"

For **critically injured** trauma patients, PHTLS sets a goal of initiating transport within **10 minutes** of EMS arrival on scene.

**Criteria for critical patient** (any one sufficient):

1. Inadequate or threatened airway
2. Impaired ventilation (RR <10 or >29, SpO₂ <90%, dyspnea, flail chest)
3. Active bleeding requiring tourniquet or wound packing with continuous pressure
4. Shock, even if compensated
5. Abnormal neurologic status (unable to follow commands, seizure, suspected spinal injury with new deficits)
6. Penetrating trauma to head, neck, torso, or proximal to elbow/knee in extremities
7. Amputation or near-amputation proximal to wrist or ankle

## Definitive Care Requirements

What trauma patients need that **cannot be provided in the field**:
1. Surgical control of internal hemorrhage
2. Blood to carry oxygen
3. Plasma for internal clotting and coagulopathy management

This is the fundamental reason why rapid transport supersedes most on-scene interventions for critically injured patients. IV lines and fluid therapy must **never delay transport**.

---

## Trauma Center Destination

The closest hospital is not always the most appropriate facility. The practitioner must consider:
- Transport time to each facility option
- Surgical capabilities (in-house vs. call-in surgeon)
- Trauma center level (I–IV; see [[trauma-centers]])
- Blood bank availability
- OR team readiness

A Level I/II trauma center with a prepared trauma team, blood bank, and an OR team immediately available can have a patient with life-threatening hemorrhage in the OR **rapidly after arrival** — that capability can make the difference between life and death.
