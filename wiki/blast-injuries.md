# Blast Injuries

**Summary**: Physics of explosions and the five-category blast injury taxonomy — primary through quinary — covering mechanisms, at-risk organs, and clinical priorities for each category.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-16

---

## Overview

Explosive devices are the most frequently used weapons in combat and terrorism. They cause injury by **multiple simultaneous mechanisms**, making triage and management more complex than single-mechanism trauma.

> "The greatest challenges for clinicians at all levels of care in the aftermath of an explosion are the large numbers of casualties and the presence of multiple penetrating injuries."
> (source: PHTLS 10th Ed., Chapter 4)

---

## Physics of Blast

An explosion is a physical, chemical, or nuclear reaction that releases large amounts of energy nearly instantaneously, in the form of:
- **Heat**
- **Rapidly expanding, highly compressed gas**
- **High-velocity fragments**

Blast waves travel at > **16,400 ft/sec (5,000 m/sec)** and have two components:

| Component | Mechanism | Effect |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Static pressure (blast overpressure)** | Surrounds objects on all sides; discontinuous pressure rise called the "shock front" | Barotrauma to gas-filled organs |
| **Dynamic pressure (blast wind)** | Directional wind following the shock front; propels fragments | Fragment injuries; body translocation |

**Pressure–time profile of a blast wave**:
1. Instantaneous rise to peak overpressure (shock front)
2. Positive phase (overpressure)
3. Pressure drops to ambient
4. Negative phase (partial vacuum as air is sucked back)

The negative phase can cause secondary structural collapse and additional injury.

**Lethal radius comparison** for a 220 lb (100 kg) charge in open space:
- Lethal blast overpressure: ~50 ft (15 m)
- Fragment injury: well over 1,000 ft (300 m)

This is why most explosive devices are designed to **maximize fragmentation** — it dramatically increases the effective damage radius.

## Enclosed vs. Open-Air Explosions

| Setting | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| **Open air** | Blast wave dissipates rapidly; most casualties from fragments and translocation; 95% of Iraq/Afghanistan blast injuries were open-space |
| **Enclosed space** | Blast wave bounces off walls and is reinforced; dramatically increases primary blast injury; higher rate of blast lung and immediate death |

## The Five Blast Injury Categories

Defined by DoD Directive 6025.21E taxonomy.

### Primary Blast Injury

**Mechanism**: Blast shock wave directly contacts the body; stress and shear waves transmitted through tissues; waves reinforced at tissue density interfaces (air–liquid junctions).

**At-risk organs**: Gas-filled structures — lungs, bowel, ears.

**Typical injuries**:
- **Tympanic membrane (TM) rupture** — most common primary blast injury; may occur at pressures as low as 5 psi (35 kPa); does NOT reliably predict lung injury
- **Blast lung (pulmonary barotrauma)** — pneumothorax, hemothorax, air embolism, interstitial emphysema, pneumomediastinum; occurs at < 40 psi (276 kPa); leading cause of immediate blast death in enclosed spaces
- Eye injuries
- Abdominal hemorrhage from bowel barotrauma
- Concussion

Injury decreases with distance and is proportionate to explosive force. Enclosed-space blasts cause more primary injury than open-air.

### Secondary Blast Injury

**Mechanism**: Ballistic wounds from fragments propelled by the blast wind. Fragments may be:
- **Primary fragments** — pieces of the weapon casing itself
- **Secondary fragments** — environmental debris (glass, concrete, nails embedded in improvised devices)

**Characteristics**:
- Most common cause of blast casualties
- Effective range far exceeds that of the blast wave
- Can produce multiple simultaneous penetrating injuries in a single patient

**Typical injuries**: Penetrating wounds, traumatic amputations, lacerations, open/closed head injuries

### Tertiary Blast Injury

**Mechanism**: Blast wind propels the body as a whole onto surfaces or objects (**whole-body translocation**); or structural collapse drives objects onto the patient (crush).

**Typical injuries**: Blunt trauma (same patterns as MVC/fall), crush syndrome, compartment syndrome, fractures

### Quaternary Blast Injury

**Mechanism**: All other explosion-related injuries not from overpressure or fragments.

**Typical injuries**:
- Burns (flame, thermal flash)
- Toxic gas and smoke inhalation
- Asphyxiation
- Injury from environmental contamination

### Quinary Blast Injury

**Mechanism**: Injuries from specific **additives** placed in the explosive device — bacteria, chemicals, radioactive materials.

**Typical injuries**:
- Chemical burns
- Bacterial infections (wound contamination)
- Radiation exposure ("dirty bomb")

## Summary Table

| Category | Cause | Organs at Risk | Examples |
|----------|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Primary** | Blast shock wave | Ears, lungs, bowel | TM rupture, blast lung, concussion |
| **Secondary** | Fragments (primary + secondary) | All — penetrating | Multiple penetrating injuries, traumatic amputation |
| **Tertiary** | Translocation / structural collapse | All — blunt | Fractures, crush, compartment syndrome |
| **Quaternary** | Heat, gas, flame | Skin, airway, lungs | Burns, inhalation injury |
| **Quinary** | Additives (bacteria, chemicals, radiation) | Variable | Wound infection, chemical burns, radiation injury |

## Multi-Etiology Injury

A single blast event can inflict all five categories on the same patient. In a vehicle-targeted IED:
- Occupants may receive minimal initial blast overpressure
- But the vehicle may flip (tertiary — blunt), be struck by fragments (secondary), catch fire (quaternary), and leave occupants exposed to ambush gunfire (penetrating) after exit

Think through all five categories systematically when assessing a blast victim.

---

## Assessment Priorities

1. **TM rupture** is not a reliable predictor of pulmonary injury — assess lungs independently
2. **Secondary fragment injuries** dominate casualties — treat as multiple penetrating wounds; fully expose and log-roll all patients
3. **Blast lung** may not be immediately apparent — monitor SpO₂ and ventilation en route; deterioration in transit is common
4. **Enclosed-space blast** should heighten suspicion for primary blast injury even when the patient appears intact
5. Relay scene information (type of device, open vs. enclosed, distance from blast) to receiving facility
