# EMS History: The Evolution of Prehospital Trauma Care

**Summary**: History of emergency medical services and prehospital trauma care from antiquity to the modern era, organized around the four periods defined by Norman McSwain, MD.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Overview

The development of prehospital trauma care can be divided into four time periods, as described by Norman McSwain, MD, in the Scudder Oration of the American College of Surgeons (2003):

1. The Ancient Period
2. The Larrey Period (~late 1700s to 1950)
3. The Farrington Era (~1950 to 1970)
4. The Modern Era (~1970 to today)

---

## 1. Ancient Period (Pre-Napoleon)

All medical care accomplished in Egypt, Greece, Rome, and by the Israelites up to the time of Napoleon is classified as premodern EMS. Most care occurred within rudimentary medical facilities; little was performed in the field.

The **Edwin Smith Papyrus** (~4,500 years ago) is the most significant surviving document — it describes medical care in a series of case reports, representing some of the earliest recorded trauma management.

---

## 2. The Larrey Period (Late 1700s to ~1950)

### Baron Dominique Jean Larrey (1766–1842)

Napoleon's chief military physician. In **1797**, he observed:

> "The remoteness of our ambulances deprive the wounded of the requisite attention. I was authorized to construct a carriage which I call flying ambulances."

Larrey developed horse-drawn **"flying ambulances"** for timely retrieval of battlefield casualties. By the early 1800s he had established the foundational elements of prehospital care:

- Rapid transport
- Proper training of medical personnel
- Movement into the field during battle for care and retrieval
- Field control of hemorrhage
- Transport to a nearby hospital
- Provision of en route care
- Development of frontline hospitals
- Field triage based on severity of injury

Larrey is recognized by many as the **father of EMS** in the modern era.

### American Civil War

Larrey's principles were initially not used by the Union Army. At the First Battle of Bull Run (August 1861), 3,000 wounded lay in the field for 3 days and 600 for up to a week.

**Jonathan Letterman** was appointed Surgeon General and created a separate medical corps. By the Second Battle of Bull Run (13 months later), 300 ambulances collected 10,000 wounded in 24 hours.

### Early US Ambulance Services

- **1864**: International Red Cross created at the First Geneva Convention — guaranteed safe passage for ambulances and medical personnel
- **1865**: First private ambulance service in the US, Cincinnati General Hospital
- **1867**: Bellevue Hospital Ambulance, New York
- **1880s**: Grady Hospital Ambulance Service, Atlanta (oldest continuously operating)
- **1885**: Charity Hospital Ambulance Services, New Orleans

Most services were run by hospitals, the military, or mortuaries until 1950.

### Nicholas Senn, MD (1891)

> "The fate of the wounded rests in the hands of one who applies the first dressing."
> — Nicholas Senn, MD, founder of the Association of Military Surgeons

## 3. The Farrington Era (~1950 to 1970)

### J. D. "Deke" Farrington, MD (1909–1982)

Known as the **father of EMS in the United States**. His landmark article **"Death in a Ditch"** (1967) stimulated the development of improved prehospital care.

Key contributions:
- Co-taught the first prehospital training course with Sam Banks, MD, to the Chicago Fire Department in **1957**
- Chaired committees that produced the *Essential Equipment List for Ambulances* (ACS Committee on Trauma), DOT ambulance design specifications, and the first EMT basic training program

### Key Documents of the Era

- **1965**: George J. Curry, MD — called for specialized training of ambulance attendants
- **1967**: *Accidental Death and Disability: The Neglected Disease of Modern Society* (NAS/NRC "white paper") — accelerated national EMS development

---

## 4. The Modern Era (~1970 to Today)

### 1970s

- **1968**: Dunlap and Associates defined the EMT-Ambulance Training curriculum for DOT
- **1970**: National Registry of EMTs (NREMT) established
- **1971**: *Emergency Care and Transportation of the Sick and Injured* ("Orange Book," AAOS, Walter Hoyt, Jr., MD) — first modern EMS textbook, now in 12th edition
- **1972**: Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) developed by Teasdale & Jennett; brought to US trauma care by Howard Champion
- **1973**: Federal EMS legislation — defined 15 components of an integrated EMS system

### 1980s

Recognition that trauma patients differ fundamentally from cardiac patients:
- For cardiac patients: CPR, defibrillation, and medications available in the field
- For trauma patients: surgical hemorrhage control and blood replacement are **not** available in the field → rapid transport becomes the priority

Concepts established in this decade:
- **Permissive hypotension** (Dr. Ken Mattox)
- **1:1 blood product ratio** (packed RBCs to plasma) — validated later in military experience (Iraq, Afghanistan)

### ATLS and PHTLS Origins

- **1976**: A private plane crash in rural Nebraska injures an orthopaedic surgeon's family. Minimal care available; wife killed, children critically injured. The surgeon recognized the need for a systematic trauma training system.
- **1978**: **Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS)** prototype field-tested in Auburn, Nebraska. Format based on ACLS; introduced "treat as you go" methodology and ABCs of trauma.
- ATLS later presented to the American College of Surgeons Committee on Trauma and became the global standard.
- **PHTLS** grew directly from ATLS — applying the same systematic approach to prehospital care.

### Military Contributions

Experience from Iraq and Afghanistan drove major advances:
- Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC)
- Prehospital use of tourniquets, hemostatic agents, blood products
- Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR)
- A military version of PHTLS first published as a revised 5th edition in 2005

### International Expansion

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| Early 1990s | PHTLS launched internationally (UK and Mexico first) |
| 2010 | NAEMT began offering TCCC Course |
| 2018 | Regional European Education Committee established |
| 2019 | 25,600+ international practitioners trained; PHTLS taught in 70+ countries |
