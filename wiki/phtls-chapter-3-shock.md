## Chapter Objectives

At completion, the learner should be able to:

- Define [[shock]] and explain its progression through phases
- Explain how preload, afterload, and contractility affect cardiac output
- Classify shock on an etiologic basis (hypovolemic, distributive, cardiogenic)
- Relate shock to acid–base status, energy production, etiology, prevention, and treatment
- Describe the physical findings of shock
- List practical assessment tools that define shock
- Clinically differentiate types of shock
- Discuss limitations of field management of shock
- Recognize the need for rapid transport and early definitive management
- Apply principles of management in the trauma patient
- List the components of the [[fick-principle]]
- Discuss limitations of [[aerobic-anaerobic-metabolism|anaerobic metabolism]] in meeting cellular demands

## Opening Scenario Summary

A 29-year-old helmeted male motorcyclist ejected 50 feet from his vehicle after striking a telephone pole. Presents with:
- Pale skin, diaphoresis, decreased peripheral pulses
- Contused chest, unstable pelvis
- Vitals: HR 110, BP 82/56 mm Hg, SpO₂ 92% on room air, RR 28
- Alert and oriented

**Management applied in scenario**: Spinal motion restriction → pelvic binder → immediate transport → supplemental O₂ (NRB) + ETCO₂ → two 18-gauge IVs en route → fluids titrated to SBP > 90 mm Hg → TXA candidate (remote location) → warmed fluids → environmental controls → radio report including anticoagulant use.

## Key Quotes and Principles

> "The fate of the wounded rests in the hands of the one who applies the first dressing." — Nicholas Senn, MD (1844–1908)

> Shock is a "rude unhinging of the machinery of life." — Samuel Gross, 1872

> A patient with signs of compensation such as tachycardia and a rising Shock Index **is already in shock**, not "going into shock."

> **Blood pressure does not drop until ≥ 30% blood loss** — it is a late sign.

> Pre-shock tourniquet application: **96% survival**. Post-shock tourniquet application: **4% survival**.

> IV lines must not delay transport. In a National Trauma Data Bank study of 776,000+ patients, prehospital IV fluid administration was associated with **increased odds of death**.

> "If the practitioner is comfortable in the patient compartment, it is too cold for the patient."

## What Changed Since Previous Editions (Notable in 10th Edition)

- Emphasis on Shock Index (SI) as a supplement to traditional vital signs
- Permissive hypotension strategy formalized with specific BP targets
- TXA endorsed with specific dosing and timing guidance
- Damage Control Resuscitation (1:1 blood product ratio) as standard
- Lyophilized plasma and prehospital blood products discussed
- Compensatory Reserve Index (CRM devices) introduced as emerging technology
- XABCDE explicitly prioritizes hemorrhage control (X) before airway (A)
