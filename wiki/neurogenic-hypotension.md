# Neurogenic Hypotension

**Summary**: Hypotension caused by spinal cord injury disrupting sympathetic tone, resulting in vasodilation below the injury level; distinguished from hypovolemic shock by warm dry skin, bradycardia, and a lucid patient.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Mechanism

Neurogenic hypotension (also called neurogenic "shock") occurs when a **cervical or upper thoracic spinal cord injury** interrupts the sympathetic nervous system pathway. The loss of sympathetic control over vascular smooth muscle causes **peripheral vasodilation below the level of injury**, dramatically increasing the vascular container size without a corresponding increase in blood volume.

Result: normal blood volume is simply insufficient to fill the expanded container → hypotension. (source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

Unlike true hemorrhagic [[shock]], tissue oxygenation usually **remains adequate** (MAP > 65) and energy production is maintained in pure neurogenic hypotension.

## Differentiating Neurogenic Hypotension from Hypovolemic Shock

| Feature | Hypovolemic Shock | Neurogenic Hypotension |
|---------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Skin temperature** | Cool, clammy | **Warm, dry** (especially below injury) |
| **Skin color** | Pale, cyanotic | **Pink** |
| **Blood pressure** | Drops | Drops |
| **Pulse** | Weak, thready, **rapid** (tachycardia) | May be weak, but **bradycardia** typical |
| **Pulse pressure** | Narrowed | Normal or **widened** |
| **Level of consciousness** | Altered (anxious → confused) | **Lucid** (in absence of TBI) |
| **Capillary refill time** | Slowed | Normal |

> The key distinguishing signs: **bradycardia + warm dry skin + lucid patient** = neurogenic hypotension.
> Bradycardia occurs because with sympathetic loss, the parasympathetic (vagal) system on the heart goes unopposed.

## Neurogenic Hypotension vs. Spinal Shock

These two terms are frequently confused:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Neurogenic hypotension** | Hemodynamic phenomenon — disruption of sympathetic nervous system → peripheral vasodilation → hypotension |
| **Spinal shock** | Neurologic phenomenon — temporary loss of all spinal cord reflex arc function below the level of injury |

They often coexist but are distinct entities. Spinal shock refers to the loss of reflexes, not hemodynamics.

## Critical Clinical Warning

Patients with neurogenic hypotension **frequently have associated injuries with significant hemorrhage**. Therefore:

> Any patient with neurogenic hypotension **plus** physical signs of hypovolemia (tachycardia, cold/clammy skin) should be treated as if hemorrhagic shock is present **first**.

Stabilization with vasopressors should only be considered **after** confirming adequate fluid resuscitation has addressed any hemorrhagic component.

## Blood Pressure Targets

- **Standard trauma resuscitation**: SBP 80–90 mm Hg acceptable for uncontrolled hemorrhage
- **Neurogenic hypotension with spinal cord injury**: Target **MAP ≥ 85–90 mm Hg** to maintain spinal cord perfusion pressure and limit secondary neurologic injury
- **TBI + hypotension**: SBP ≥ 110 mm Hg

## Management Approach

1. Treat associated hemorrhage first
2. Spinal motion restriction
3. Administer IV fluids to restore adequate preload
4. If hypotension persists after adequate volume resuscitation, consider vasopressors (per medical direction)
5. Left uterine displacement if pregnant (to relieve IVC compression from gravid uterus)
