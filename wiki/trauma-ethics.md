# Medical Ethics in Prehospital Trauma Care

**Summary**: Application of the four principles of biomedical ethics (principlism) — autonomy, nonmaleficence, beneficence, and justice — to prehospital trauma care, plus informed consent, privacy, confidentiality, and truth telling.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Four Principles (Principlism)

The ethical framework most commonly applied in medicine uses four principles to weigh and balance benefits and burdens in the care of a specific patient.

| Principle | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Autonomy** | The patient's right to direct their own health care |
| **Nonmaleficence** | The obligation not to take actions likely to harm the patient |
| **Beneficence** | The obligation to maximize benefits and minimize risks to the patient |
| **Justice** | Fair distribution of medical resources |

These principles often conflict in trauma scenarios, requiring active balancing rather than rigid application.

---

## Autonomy

Patients have the right to make decisions about their own medical care — including the right to **refuse treatment**.

Complications in trauma:
- Brain injury, shock, or intoxication may impair decision-making capacity
- Eligible surrogates are often unavailable at the scene
- The practitioner should explain the situation to patients whenever possible, in a way that allows them to make informed decisions

Autonomy does not disappear because a patient is injured. Even when full disclosure of information is not possible, the practitioner should preserve as much patient autonomy as the clinical situation allows.

---

## Nonmaleficence

"First, do no harm."

Examples in prehospital trauma:
- Limiting unnecessary spinal motion in a patient with high-energy trauma and respiratory difficulty (risk of harm from immobilization must be weighed against risk of harm from spinal movement)
- Not administering IV fluids that may worsen coagulopathy in penetrating hemorrhagic shock
- Not delaying transport to perform interventions with marginal benefit

A practical example of balancing nonmaleficence and beneficence: A morbidly obese patient with thoracolumbar injury needs head elevation to breathe. Elevating the spine board by 30° at the head end (or reverse Trendelenburg) satisfies both the need to restrict motion and the need to improve breathing — neither principle is abandoned.

---

## Beneficence

"Do good."

Requires practitioners to act in a manner that **maximizes benefits and minimizes risks** to the patient. In practice, this means:
- Performing interventions only when the anticipated benefit justifies the risk and time cost
- Choosing the intervention most likely to improve outcome for this specific patient (see [[principles-vs-preferences]])
- Not withholding beneficial treatments based solely on age or other demographic factors

---

## Justice

Fair distribution of medical resources.

In everyday trauma care, justice considerations include resource allocation decisions (e.g., directing the only available unit to the highest-acuity patient).

In **mass-casualty incidents (MCIs)**, justice must be weighed differently:
- Triage prioritizes based on probability of survival
- The sickest or most critically injured patients may be moved to an "expectant" category when resources are overwhelmed
- What is most just in a specific situation depends on the availability of resources and the fairest way to use them

## Informed Consent

Informed consent is the process by which a practitioner provides a patient (or surrogate) with the information needed to consent to or refuse treatment.

### Requirements for Valid Informed Consent

The patient must:
1. Have decision-making capacity
2. Be able to communicate their understanding of their diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment options
3. Give consent or refusal **voluntarily**
4. Actively refuse or consent to treatment

### Decision-Making Capacity vs. Competence

| Term | Domain | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Competence** | Legal | General ability to make good decisions for oneself |
| **Decision-making capacity** | Clinical | Ability to make decisions regarding a specific set of medical treatment options |

Assessing capacity is especially difficult in acute trauma: baseline is often unknown, the patient is acutely injured, and intoxication or shock may cloud the picture.

### When Informed Consent May Be Overridden (Emergency Exception)

All three conditions must apply:
1. The patient lacks decision-making capacity (unconscious or significantly impaired) and no surrogate is available
2. The condition is potentially life- or health-threatening and the patient may suffer irreversible damage without treatment
3. A reasonable person would consent to the treatment

In these circumstances, a healthcare practitioner may proceed with treatment under **implied consent**.

### Refusal of Care

A patient with intact decision-making capacity has the right to refuse treatment. This right must be respected even when the practitioner disagrees with the decision.

The intoxicated, belligerent patient presents a challenge: intoxication may impair decision-making capacity. The practitioner must assess whether the patient is truly capable of an autonomous refusal — this is a clinical judgment, not a simple yes/no.

## Privacy and Confidentiality

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Privacy** | The patient's right to control who has access to their personal health information |
| **Confidentiality** | The practitioner's obligation not to inappropriately share information disclosed in the patient–practitioner relationship |

Patient information should not be disclosed to:
- Observers at the scene
- News media
- Non-treating family members without patient authorization

Appropriate disclosures include:
- Other medical professionals involved in the patient's care
- Agencies responsible for mandated reporting (e.g., child or elder abuse)

Even when gathering a history from family members or bystanders (when the patient is incapacitated), great care should be taken to protect patient privacy.

## Truth Telling

Truthfulness is both a professional obligation and the foundation of a trusting patient–practitioner relationship.

### Challenges in Trauma

- Multivictim scenarios: a survivor may ask about the condition of a critically injured or non-surviving loved one
- The information, if disclosed fully and immediately, may cause significant harm (emotional shock, impaired decision-making capacity, harm to the 5-year-old child who is conscious and nearby)

### The Limits of Immediate Disclosure

> "It is never acceptable to lie to a patient. But withholding, or more accurately, **delaying** communication of certain sensitive information may at times be necessary in the context of prioritizing lifesaving care over communicating distressing information to a patient who may be unprepared to fully process it."

The obligation to tell the truth remains — it may be fulfilled at a more appropriate moment rather than the most acute phase of care. This is not permission to deceive; it is recognition that timing matters.

---

## Ethical Complexity in the Field

> "Ethics rarely gives black-and-white solutions to difficult situations. Rather, ethics can provide a framework in which to consider and reason through ethically difficult situations in an attempt to do the right thing."

The four principles (autonomy, nonmaleficence, beneficence, justice) are tools for reasoning through conflict, not algorithms that produce automatic answers. The practitioner must weigh the principles against each other in the context of the specific patient, the specific moment, and the available resources.
