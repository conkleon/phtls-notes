# Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

**Summary**: An antifibrinolytic medication that stabilizes blood clots by blocking the breakdown of fibrin; indicated within 3 hours of injury for trauma patients with suspected hemorrhagic shock or massive hemorrhage.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Mechanism of Action

When the coagulation cascade is activated to form a clot, the fibrinolytic process (clot breakdown) begins simultaneously. In severe trauma, fibrinolysis can occur too rapidly, dissolving clots before hemorrhage is controlled.

TXA works by:
1. **Binding to plasminogen** — preventing its conversion to plasmin
2. **Blocking plasmin** from breaking down fibrin in a formed clot
3. **Stabilizing the clot** → reduces ongoing hemorrhage

TXA also has an **anti-inflammatory effect**. (source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

## Pharmacology

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Drug class** | Lysine analog / antifibrinolytic |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits plasminogen → plasmin conversion; prevents fibrin degradation |
| **Routes** | IV or IO |
| **Dose** | **2 grams given slowly** (IV/IO push) |
| **Timing window** | Must be given **within 3 hours of injury** — benefit diminishes rapidly after this |

## Indications (Prehospital — Tactical/Military Guidelines)

TXA should be considered for patients who:
- Will likely require a **blood transfusion** (hemorrhagic shock, elevated lactate, major amputation, penetrating torso trauma, evidence of severe bleeding), **OR**
- Have signs of **significant TBI** (altered mental status associated with blast or blunt trauma)

AND present **no later than 3 hours after injury**.

> Current tactical casualty care guidelines (military and civilian tactical EMS) endorse a one-time dose of 2g TXA IV/IO.

## Evidence Base

- **CRASH-2 trial**: demonstrated survival improvement in severely injured trauma patients receiving TXA within 3 hours
- Military experience (Iraq/Afghanistan): supported early TXA in combat casualties
- Most effective when given **early** (< 1 hour from injury) and when patients are **severely injured** (hypotensive, tachycardic)
- Not all studies show definitive benefit, particularly for TBI — research ongoing

> TXA appears most beneficial in hypotensive, tachycardic patients with high likelihood of requiring transfusion.

## Timing Is Critical

TXA becomes **potentially harmful** if given >3 hours after injury, as late administration may increase the risk of thrombotic complications and has not been shown to improve survival.

## Prior Medical Use

TXA has been used for decades in:
- Gynecologic patients with severe uterine bleeding
- Cardiac and orthopedic surgery
- Hemophiliac patients undergoing dental procedures

Its expansion to prehospital trauma care represents translation of established pharmacology to a new application.
