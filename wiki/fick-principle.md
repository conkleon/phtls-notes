# Fick Principle

**Summary**: The Fick principle describes the three components required for tissue oxygenation — O₂ on-loading in the lungs, delivery via RBCs to tissues, and off-loading at the cellular level — providing the physiologic framework for understanding and treating shock.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Formula

> **VO₂ = CO × (CaO₂ − CvO₂)**

| Variable | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| **VO₂** | Oxygen consumption (mL O₂/min) — index of the body's ability to do work |
| **CO** | Cardiac output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume |
| **CaO₂** | Oxygen concentration in arterial blood |
| **CvO₂** | Oxygen concentration in venous blood |

CaO₂ = (SaO₂ × 1.36 × Hb) + (0.003 × PaO₂)

Normal VO₂ at rest: 35–40 mL/kg/min (male), 27–30 mL/kg/min (female). (source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

## Three Components of Oxygen Delivery

**1. On-loading O₂ to RBCs in the lungs**
- Requires a patent airway and adequate ventilation
- Covered in detail in PHTLS Chapter 7 (Airway and Ventilation)
- Impaired by: airway obstruction, pneumothorax, pulmonary contusion, apnea

**2. Delivery of oxygenated RBCs to tissue cells**
- Requires adequate cardiac output and circulating RBC mass
- Impaired by: hemorrhage (reduced RBCs), cardiogenic shock (low CO), tamponade, tension pneumothorax
- A helpful analogy: RBCs = train cars, lungs = loading station, blood vessels = railway, tissue cells = train stops

**3. Off-loading O₂ from RBCs to tissue cells**
- Requires adequate capillary perfusion and normal cellular environment
- Impaired by: hypothermia (increases hemoglobin affinity for O₂, reducing off-loading), interstitial edema (increases diffusion distance), acidosis

## Mixed Venous Oxygen Saturation (SvO₂)

Under normal conditions, only **25% of available oxygen is extracted** at the tissues; approximately 75% returns in venous blood. SvO₂ (measured in hospital) reflects this. A low SvO₂ indicates:
- Increased oxygen consumption (e.g., sepsis, agitation), or
- Decreased oxygen supply (e.g., hemorrhage, low CO)

## Clinical Relevance to Shock

A patient can have a normal SpO₂ on pulse oximetry and still be in severe [[shock]] if:
- Cardiac output is dramatically reduced (fewer RBC deliveries per minute)
- RBC mass is reduced by hemorrhage (less oxygen per delivery)
- Hypothermia prevents off-loading at the cellular level

**Example**: A hemorrhaging patient with 50% blood volume loss may show SpO₂ 99% because the remaining blood is fully saturated — but total oxygen *delivery* is halved.

## Prehospital Interventions Targeting Each Component

| Component | Intervention |
|-----------|-------------|
| On-loading | Airway management, supplemental O₂ (FiO₂ close to 1.0), assisted ventilation |
| Delivery | Hemorrhage control, fluid resuscitation, blood products, maintain CO |
| Off-loading | Prevent hypothermia, avoid over-resuscitation with crystalloids (prevents edema) |

The primary survey (XABCDE) and management priorities in [[shock-management]] are structured around maintaining all three Fick components simultaneously.
