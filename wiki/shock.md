# Shock

**Summary**: Shock is a state of inadequate cellular perfusion causing a shift from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism; it is defined at the cellular level, not by vital signs alone.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Definition

Shock is **insufficient tissue perfusion at the cellular level**, leading to anaerobic metabolism and loss of energy production needed to sustain life. It is *not* defined as low blood pressure, rapid pulse, or cool clammy skin — those are systemic manifestations of the underlying process. (source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

The term derives from the French *choc*. In 1872, surgeon Samuel Gross described it as "a rude unhinging of the machinery of life."

## Core Pathophysiology

Normal cellular function depends on **aerobic metabolism**: oxygen + glucose → 38 ATP + H₂O + CO₂.

When oxygen delivery fails, cells switch to **anaerobic metabolism**: glucose → 2 ATP + lactic acid. This yields 19× less energy and produces metabolic acidosis. When pH falls below 7.20, myocardial contraction is severely depressed.

If anaerobic metabolism is not reversed:
1. Cells die
2. Organs fail
3. Patient dies

Time from hypoperfusion to irreversible damage depends on the organ — see [[aerobic-anaerobic-metabolism]].

## Classification of Traumatic Shock

| Type | Mechanism | Most Common Cause in Trauma |
|------|-----------|----------------------------|
| **Hypovolemic** | Fluid/blood loss reduces circulating volume | Hemorrhage (most common overall) |
| **Distributive (Vasogenic)** | Vascular container enlarges without volume increase | Spinal cord injury → [[neurogenic-hypotension]] |
| **Cardiogenic** | Pump failure | Cardiac contusion, tamponade, tension pneumothorax |

**Hemorrhage is the most common cause of shock in trauma. Assume hemorrhagic until proven otherwise.**

## Compensated vs. Decompensated Shock

- **Compensated shock**: Sympathetic compensation (tachycardia, vasoconstriction) maintains blood pressure. Signs include tachycardia, tachypnea, and narrowing pulse pressure — BP may appear normal.
- **Decompensated shock**: Compensatory mechanisms fail; blood pressure drops. This is a **late sign** and indicates impending death. A falling BP means the patient has already lost ≥30% blood volume.

> A patient with signs of tachycardia and a rising Shock Index **is already in shock**, not "going into shock."

## Shock Index (SI)

SI = Heart Rate ÷ Systolic Blood Pressure

- Normal: < 0.7
- Concerning: ≥ 0.9 (associated with higher mortality and critical bleeding risk)
- Severe: > 1.0 (Class III hemorrhage or worse)

SI is a more sensitive early indicator of shock than blood pressure alone.

## Cardiovascular Physiology

The cardiovascular system maintains perfusion via:

- **Preload**: volume entering the right atrium (Starling's law — more stretch → stronger contraction, to a point)
- **Afterload**: systemic vascular resistance the left ventricle must overcome
- **Contractility**: intrinsic pump strength
- **Heart rate**: CO = HR × Stroke Volume; normal CO = 5–6 L/min

**MAP** (Mean Arterial Pressure) = DBP + ⅓ Pulse Pressure, or (2×DBP + SBP) ÷ 3. Normal 70–100 mm Hg. More reliable indicator of end-organ perfusion than SBP alone.

## Body Fluid Compartments

| Compartment | % Body Weight |
|-------------|--------------|
| Intracellular | 45% |
| Interstitial | 10.5% |
| Intravascular | 4.5% |

Fluid movement across capillaries is governed by hydrostatic pressure, oncotic pressure, and capillary permeability — all altered in shock. Over-resuscitation with crystalloids increases interstitial edema, impairing oxygen delivery.
