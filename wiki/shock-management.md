# Shock Management

**Summary**: Prehospital management of traumatic shock prioritizes hemorrhage control, oxygenation, and rapid transport over IV access and fluid resuscitation; volume strategy is tailored to injury type, with permissive hypotension for uncontrolled hemorrhage and higher BP targets for TBI/spinal injury.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Five Steps of Shock Management

1. **Control any external severe hemorrhage**
2. **Ensure oxygenation and ventilation** (airway management)
3. **Identify the source of hemorrhage** (control external, recognize internal)
4. **Transport to definitive care** — do not delay for IV access
5. **Administer blood component therapy** when appropriate

(source: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf)

## Hemorrhage Control

Control of bleeding is the **first priority** — address before IV lines, before fluid, before anything else.

### Hierarchy of External Hemorrhage Control

1. **Direct hand pressure** over bleeding site
2. **Pressure dressing** with wound packing
3. **Tourniquet** (if direct pressure fails on extremity)
4. **Junctional tourniquet** (groin, axilla, neck wounds)
5. **Pelvic binder** for suspected pelvic fractures

### Direct Pressure
- Increases extramural (extraluminal) pressure → reduces transmural pressure → slows blood loss
- Also compresses vessel walls → reduces effective hole size
- **Critical points**:
  - Apply pressure *into* the wound, not just on the skin surface
  - Impaled objects: pressure on either *side*, do not remove (may be tamponading)
  - A pressure dressing replaces direct manual pressure for transport
  - **IV lines must not take priority over direct pressure on a hemorrhaging wound**

Transmural pressure = Intramural pressure − Extramural pressure

### Tourniquets
- Indicated when direct pressure fails for **extremity hemorrhage**
- Military data (Iraq/Afghanistan): no limbs lost due to properly applied tourniquets; 80%+ effective at controlling hemorrhage
- Pre-shock tourniquet = **96% survival**; post-shock tourniquet = **4% survival** — apply early, before BP drops
- **Application site**: groin (lower extremity) or axilla (upper extremity) — most proximal effective site
- **Tightness**: occlude arterial flow and distal pulse — venous-only occlusion *worsens* hemorrhage
- **Time**: safe for up to 150 minutes in OR; leave in place until definitive care
- **Two tourniquets side by side** if first fails to stop bleeding
- Mark time of application (e.g., "TK 2145")
- Do not routinely loosen every 10–15 min — increases blood loss without benefit
- **Recommend approved devices**: C-A-T Gen 6/7, EMT, SOFTT-W, TMT, RMT-T, SAM-XT, TPT2

### Hemostatic Agents
For wounds not amenable to tourniquet (abdomen, groin, junctional areas):
- **Combat Gauze, ChitoGauze, Celox**: pack tightly into wound; maintain direct pressure ≥ 3 min (hemostatic agent) or 10 min (plain gauze)
- **XStat**: multiple small hemostatic sponges injected into narrow-tract junctional wounds; do not remove in field
- **iTClamp**: temporary wound closure for tamponade effect
- Pack **into the base of the wound** — covering the wound surface is not enough

### Junctional Hemorrhage
Wounds at groin, axilla, neck cannot accommodate standard tourniquets. Options:
- **CRoC** (Combat Ready Clamp)
- **JETT** (Junctional Emergency Treatment Tool)
- **SJT** (SAM Junctional Tourniquet)
- Limited civilian evidence; primarily from military combat use

### Pelvic Binders
- Suspect pelvic fracture: instability, leg-length inequality, pelvic pain, perineal ecchymosis
- Binders splint and approximate fractures, limiting retroperitoneal hemorrhage
- No studies showing outcome change in prehospital setting, but sound physiologic rationale and no evidence of harm

## Airway and Breathing

- All trauma patients in shock (or at risk): **supplemental O₂ as close to 100% FiO₂ as possible** (requires reservoir device — nasal cannula or simple mask insufficient)
- Target **SpO₂ ≥ 94%**
- **Avoid hyperventilation**: increases intrathoracic pressure → impairs venous return → hypotension; in TBI, causes cerebral vasoconstriction → worsened secondary injury
- Reasonable adult ventilation: **350–500 mL tidal volume at 10 breaths/min**
- ETCO₂ monitoring useful for tube position confirmation, but correlation with PaCO₂ unreliable in shock

## Circulation: Vascular Access

### Guiding Principle
> Transport should **never be delayed** to establish IV access. IV lines are placed in the ambulance *en route*.

No prehospital study has shown improved survival from prehospital IV fluid administration. Multiple studies showed no benefit from crystalloids before hemorrhage was controlled; one study of >776,000 patients found prehospital IV fluid was associated with **increased odds of death**.

### IV Route
- Two large-bore (18-gauge or larger), short (1-inch/25-mm) catheters
- Preferred site: forearm veins
- Flow rate ∝ radius⁴ / length — wider and shorter = much faster flow
- Alternative: antecubital fossa, hand, cephalic vein

### Intraosseous (IO) Route
- Valid alternative when peripheral IV unobtainable
- Sites: proximal/distal tibia, distal femur, **humeral head** (best flow), sternum (specific devices required)
- Pain management required for conscious patients
- Consider in delayed/prolonged transport

## Volume Resuscitation

### Blood Products (First Choice for Severe Hemorrhagic Shock)
- **Whole blood** ideal — replaces O₂ capacity, clotting factors, oncotic proteins simultaneously
- **PRBCs + FFP at 1:1 or 1:2 ratio** (reconstituted whole blood)
- Add platelets and cryoprecipitate as needed
- Prehospital blood increasingly available (some EMS/HEMS systems carry thawed plasma or lyophilized plasma)
- **Lyophilized plasma**: 2-year shelf life, no refrigeration required, must be reconstituted — used in several countries
- Prehospital thawed plasma: one study showed significant improvement in 30-day mortality in 500+ patients

### Isotonic Crystalloids
- **Lactated Ringer's**: preferred (most similar to plasma electrolyte composition)
- **Normal saline (0.9% NaCl)**: alternative; large volumes → hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis
- **Plasma-Lyte, Normosol**: balanced alternatives, less renal dysfunction than NS in critically ill
- **D5W**: NOT an effective volume expander — no role in trauma resuscitation
- Crystalloids distribute to interstitium within 30–60 min — only 25–33% remains intravascular; excess causes edema impeding O₂ diffusion

### Hypertonic Saline (7.5% NaCl)
- 250 mL produces effect equivalent to 2–3 L isotonic crystalloid
- Putative anti-inflammatory/anti-infective effects in experimental models
- **No demonstrated survival benefit** over isotonic crystalloids in clinical trials
- Not routinely recommended prehospital

### Synthetic Colloids (Hetastarch/Dextran)
- Draw interstitial fluid into intravascular space
- Do not transport oxygen
- Associated with acute kidney injury and increased mortality (meta-analyses)
- **Not recommended** for prehospital shock management

## Volume Resuscitation Strategy (Algorithm)

### Uncontrolled Hemorrhage (Internal: Chest / Abdomen / Pelvis)
**Target: SBP 80–90 mm Hg (MAP 60–65 mm Hg)**
- Small boluses, avoid overshoot
- "Permissive hypotension" / "hypotensive resuscitation" / "balanced resuscitation"
- Rationale: higher BP dislodges clots, dilutes clotting factors, increases internal bleeding

### Controlled Hemorrhage (External Bleeding Stopped)
- Adult Class II–IV: initial bolus 250 mL crystalloid, repeat up to 1 L total, target SBP ≥ 90 mm Hg
- Pediatric: 20 mL/kg warmed crystalloid
- Monitor response to first bolus:
  - **Rapid response** (vital signs normalize and stay): lost < 20% blood volume, hemorrhage stopped → maintain normal vital signs
  - **Transient response** (initially improves then deteriorates): ongoing hemorrhage, 20–40% blood loss → permissive hypotension, limit crystalloid
  - **Minimal/no response**: massive hemorrhage, ongoing → immediate surgery needed, permissive hypotension

### CNS Injuries (TBI or Spinal Cord Injury)
**Target: SBP ≥ 110 mm Hg** (Brain Trauma Foundation guidelines)
- For spinal cord injury: **MAP ≥ 85–90 mm Hg**
- More aggressive resuscitation at expense of increased bleeding risk
- Hypotension + TBI: dramatically increased mortality (> 40% in one study)

### Keep Vein Open (KVO)
For Class I shock or no active hemorrhage: ~30 mL/hr (just to maintain access)

## Temperature Management

- **Warm all IV fluids** to 102°F (39°C)
- Remove wet/blood-soaked clothing
- Cover with blankets or plastic sheeting
- Ambulance patient compartment ≥ 85°F (29°C)
- *"If the practitioner is comfortable, it is too cold for the patient"*

---

## Patient Positioning

- **Standard transport position**: supine
- **Trendelenburg** (head-down): NOT recommended — aggravates ventilation, aspiration risk, increases ICP
- **Isolated TBI**: 30-degree head-of-bed elevation (reverse Trendelenburg) → improves cerebral perfusion pressure, reduces ICP
- **If intubated**: 30-degree incline reduces aspiration and VAP risk — must elevate head of stretcher, not flex patient at waist

## Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

- Candidate: suspected massive hemorrhage within 3 hours of injury, or significant TBI
- Dose: **2g IV/IO push (slowly)**
- See [[tranexamic-acid]] for full details

## Disability Management

- No specific prehospital intervention for altered mental status from shock
- Correcting hypoxia and restoring perfusion → mental status improves
- GCS score should be documented after resuscitation is underway — GCS in shock may falsely worsen prognosis

## Complications to Know (Post-Shock)

| Complication | Mechanism |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Acute Renal Failure (ATN)** | Renal tubular ischemia > 45–60 min → tubular necrosis |
| **ARDS** | Alveolar cell damage + crystalloid overload → pulmonary edema (non-cardiogenic); historically "Da Nang lung" |
| **Coagulopathy** | Part of [[lethal-triad]]; clotting factor depletion/hypothermia/acidosis |
| **Hepatic Failure** | Elevated LFTs days later; impairs clotting factor synthesis |
| **Overwhelming Infection** | WBC decrease, gut bacterial translocation, immune impairment |
| **MODS** | Each additional failing organ increases mortality; 4 organ systems = ~100% mortality |

## Prolonged Transport Considerations

- Optimize airway before long transport; intubate if airway patency questionable
- Reasonable tidal volume and rate to avoid auto-PEEP and preload compromise
- Continuous SpO₂ and capnography monitoring
- External bleeding: convert tourniquet to dressing if transport > 4 hours (slowly loosen while watching for re-bleeding)
  - **Do NOT convert** if: Class III/IV hemorrhage, complete amputation, cannot observe patient, tourniquet > 6 hours
- Urine output monitoring via catheter if local protocols permit:
  - Adults: ≥ 0.5 mL/kg/hr
  - Pediatric: ≥ 1 mL/kg/hr
  - Infants < 1 year: ≥ 2 mL/kg/hr
- Warm ALL fluids; warm patient compartment
- Consider nasogastric/orogastric tube for intubated patients to prevent gastric distension
