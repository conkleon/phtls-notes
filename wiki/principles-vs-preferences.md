# Principles vs. Preferences in Prehospital Trauma Care

**Summary**: The foundational PHTLS decision-making framework distinguishing what must be accomplished (principles, driven by science) from how it is accomplished (preferences, the art of medicine) — and the five factors that shape preferences in the field.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Core Distinction

> "The science of medicine provides the basis for the **principles** of medical care. Simply stated, principles define what the prehospital care practitioner must accomplish to maximize the chance of patient survival with the best possible outcome. How these principles are implemented by the individual practitioner to most efficiently manage the patient depends on the **preferences**."

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Principle** | What must be achieved — the scientific goal | Air containing oxygen must reach the alveoli |
| **Preference** | How it is achieved — the chosen method | Bag-mask vs. NPA vs. ET intubation vs. cricothyroidotomy |

Both principle and preference together represent the **science and art** of medicine.

---

## Illustrative Example: Airway Management

**The principle**: Air containing oxygen must be moved through an open airway into the alveoli of the lungs to facilitate oxygen–carbon dioxide exchange with red blood cells so they may deliver oxygen to other tissues.

This principle is **universal** — it never changes.

**The preference** varies by:

| Practitioner | Likely Preferred Method |
|-------------|------------------------|
| Bystander (CPR training only) | Mouth-to-mask ventilation |
| EMT | Oral airway + bag-mask ventilation |
| Paramedic (comfortable with BVM) | Bag-mask device + rapid transport |
| Paramedic (intubation trained) | Endotracheal tube, if indicated and authorized |
| Military Corpsman (in active fire) | Cricothyroidotomy or nothing until safe |
| ED Physician | Paralytic drugs + fiber-optic guided ET tube |

None of these choices is wrong for its specific context; none is correct all of the time.

## The Five Preference Factors

These five factors interact to determine which preference is most appropriate for a given patient at a given moment:

### 1. Scene Situation
The physical and tactical context in which care is delivered.

- Is the scene safe? (see [[golden-principles]] — Principle 1)
- Combat vs. civilian EMS vs. wilderness vs. tactical EMS (TEMS)
- Active fire, active shooter, chemical hazard, etc.

**TCCC three-step model** (military equivalent):
1. Care under fire/threat — minimal care while actively threatened
2. Tactical field care — after shooting stops, some danger remains
3. Tactical evacuation care — safe environment, full assessment

In a fully-involved house fire: **no assessment**, drag the patient to safety first, **then** assess.

### 2. Condition of the Patient

The clinical picture determines urgency and narrows acceptable options.

Key questions:
- How sick is this patient?
- What is their physiologic status (HR, BP, RR, skin signs)?
- What is the mechanism of injury?
- What medications or substances are on board?
- Pediatric, geriatric, pregnant, morbidly obese?

### 3. Fund of Knowledge of the Practitioner

Skills deteriorate without practice. The comfort level with any technical skill depends on frequency of performance.

Practitioners must honestly assess:
- When did I last perform this skill on a live patient?
- Am I proficient enough to attempt this under field conditions?
- Will attempting this advanced skill delay transport and worsen outcome?

> "Without the appropriate skills and experience, the patient would likely be better off if you were to choose a nasopharyngeal or oropharyngeal airway plus bag-mask device rather than endotracheal intubation."

The option that provides the fastest airway control with the least associated risk is the correct choice — regardless of how advanced it is.

### 4. Local Protocols

Protocols define the scope of practice for each certification level and under what circumstances specific interventions are authorized.

- Protocols cannot describe how to care for every patient
- They guide the approach in a way consistent with best practices, local resources, and training
- Protocols may authorize specific procedures (e.g., rapid-sequence induction) or mandate specific transport destinations

Even within protocols, the practitioner must apply critical thinking — protocols are guides, not substitutes for understanding.

### 5. Equipment Available

The best intervention is meaningless without the equipment to perform it.

- Blood may be the ideal resuscitation fluid but is frequently unavailable in the field → crystalloid becomes the preference
- Consider whether permissive hypotension (no fluids or minimal fluids) may be more appropriate than crystalloid resuscitation for penetrating trauma (see [[shock-management]])

## Knowledge-Driven vs. Protocol-Driven Care

A core PHTLS philosophy:

> "Patient care should be **knowledge-driven** and not purely **protocol-driven**."

This does not mean ignoring protocols. It means:
- Protocols must be guided by an understanding of anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology
- When a protocol does not fit the patient in front of you, understanding why the protocol was written allows you to reason through the best alternative
- Critical thinking replaces cookbook medicine

---

## Critical Thinking in Practice

Critical thinking in prehospital care means:
1. Recognizing what you know and what you don't know
2. Applying your fund of knowledge to the specific patient in front of you
3. Asking: "Is what I am doing going to benefit this patient? Does that benefit outweigh the risk of delaying transport?"
4. Updating your plan as the patient's condition evolves

Critical thinking skills improve with experience, review, and deliberate practice — similar to a surgeon or a pilot.
