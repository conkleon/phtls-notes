# Trauma Centers: ACS Levels and the Trauma System

**Summary**: Overview of the American College of Surgeons (ACS) trauma center designation system — Levels I through IV — and the role each plays in the regional trauma care network.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Overview

The American College of Surgeons (ACS) establishes the requirements for trauma centers in a document called *Resources for Optimal Care of the Injured Patient*. State and local jurisdictions use these requirements to **designate** trauma centers at varying levels, informed by the ACS Committee on Trauma (COT) Verification Review Committee.

> **Important**: The ACS does **not** designate which institutions are trauma centers. They only **verify** that hospitals have or have not met the recommended criteria. Actual designation lies with **state and local government**.

---

## Trauma Center Levels

| Level | Key Characteristics |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Level I** | Highest resource level; highest patient volume; provides medical education, research, and specialty services; serves as the regional hub |
| **Level II** | Same clinical requirements as Level I; typically lower volume, less research/education emphasis |
| **Level III** | Fewer resources; usually suburban or rural; primary role: immediate treatment, stabilization, and efficient transfer to Level I/II |
| **Level IV** | Minimal resources; 24-hour staffed ED; provides immediate basic care and stabilization; major role is rapid transfer to a higher-level center |

### Level I vs. Level II

There should be **no clinical difference** in care capabilities between Level I and Level II. The distinction is primarily:
- Medical education and research (higher at Level I)
- Specialty services availability
- Patient volume (higher at Level I)
- Role as regional hub (Level I)

## What Makes a Trauma Center Capable?

For a critically injured patient with life-threatening hemorrhage, the key resources are:

1. **Trained and prepared trauma surgeon** — available at or shortly after patient arrival
2. **Well-trained trauma-experienced resuscitation team** — ED nurses, emergency physicians
3. **OR team immediately available** — ready to take patient directly from ED to OR
4. **Blood bank** — immediate access to packed RBCs, plasma, platelets
5. **Trauma ICU** — for damage control resuscitation and staged reconstruction

A trauma center with all of these resources can have a patient with life-threatening hemorrhage in the OR **rapidly after arrival** — that speed can determine survival.

## Impact on Prehospital Transport Decisions

The prehospital practitioner must determine **both**:
1. The urgency of the patient's condition (see [[golden-period]] — Platinum 10 Minutes)
2. The most appropriate destination facility

The closest hospital is **not** always the most appropriate. A hospital without in-house surgical capabilities must await surgeon arrival before taking the patient to the OR — adding critical minutes of uncontrolled hemorrhage.

Key consideration: **"How long until this patient can be in the OR at each available facility?"**

For critically injured patients, this almost always favors direct transport to a Level I or II trauma center, bypassing a closer non-trauma hospital or lower-level facility if transport time difference is acceptable.

## Trauma System Components

An integrated trauma system requires:
- A single emergency phone number (e.g., 9-1-1)
- A good communications system for dispatch
- Well-prepared and well-trained prehospital care practitioners
- Designated trauma centers at appropriate levels
- Triage protocols for field trauma center designation
- Quality assurance and registry processes

The federal EMS legislation of 1973 identified 15 individual components needed for an integrated EMS system. Trauma center designation is one component of a broader, coordinated trauma system.

## Mechanism-Based Triage to Trauma Centers

The following mechanisms warrant consideration for direct triage to a trauma center (from ACS Field Triage Guidelines):

**Falls**:
- Greater than 10 feet (3 meters) for any age

**High-risk auto crashes**:
- Intrusion >12 inches at occupant site, or >18 inches at any site
- Need for extrication
- Ejection (partial or complete) from automobile
- Death in same passenger compartment
- Vehicle telemetry consistent with high-risk injury
- Pedestrian/cyclist thrown, run over, or with significant impact
- Rider separated from motorcycle/ATV/horse with significant impact
