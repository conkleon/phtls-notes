## Chapter Objectives

At completion, the learner should be able to:

- Describe the difference between principles and preferences in relation to decision making in the field
- Given a trauma scenario, discuss the principles of trauma care for the specific situation
- Use critical-thinking skills to determine the preferred method for accomplishing principles
- Relate the four principles of ethical decision making to prehospital trauma care
- Discuss the Golden Period and why trauma patients die
- Discuss the 14 "Golden Principles" of prehospital trauma care
- Identify the components and importance of prehospital research and literature

## Opening Scenario

A two-vehicle T-bone collision with six patients: an intoxicated belligerent unrestrained male driver with open arm fracture; an older adult female passenger (unresponsive, not breathing); a conscious but distressed female sedan driver; a 3-year-old unconscious child in a car seat; and a 5-year-old crying but apparently uninjured child. The scenario is used throughout the chapter to illustrate principles, preferences, ethics, and critical thinking.

## Core Concept: Principles vs. Preferences

> "The science of medicine provides the basis for the **principles** of medical care. Simply stated, principles define what the prehospital care practitioner must accomplish to maximize the chance of patient survival... How these principles are implemented depends on the **preferences**."

The five **preference factors** that determine how a principle is applied:
1. Scene situation
2. Condition of the patient
3. Fund of knowledge of the practitioner
4. Local protocols
5. Equipment available

See [[principles-vs-preferences]] for full framework.

## Medical Ethics Summary

Four principles of **principlism** applied to trauma care:
- **Autonomy** — patient's right to direct their own health care
- **Nonmaleficence** — obligation not to harm the patient
- **Beneficence** — act to maximize benefits and minimize risks
- **Justice** — fair distribution of medical resources

See [[trauma-ethics]] for informed consent, privacy, confidentiality, and truth-telling.

## The 14 Golden Principles (Summary)

| # | Principle |
|---|-----------|
| 1 | Ensure safety of practitioners and patient |
| 2 | Assess the scene for need for additional resources |
| 3 | Control any significant external hemorrhage |
| 4 | Use the primary survey (XABCDE) to identify life-threatening conditions |
| 5 | Recognize the physics of trauma |
| 6 | Airway management with spinal motion restriction as indicated |
| 7 | Support ventilation; maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94% |
| 8 | Basic shock therapy: splinting, normothermia |
| 9 | Apply appropriate spinal motion restriction |
| 10 | Transport critically injured patients within 10 minutes ("Platinum 10") |
| 11 | Initiate fluid replacement en route |
| 12 | Secondary survey after life threats managed |
| 13 | Provide adequate pain relief |
| 14 | Thorough communication with receiving facility |

See [[golden-principles]] for full detail on each.

## Key Quotes

> "Initiation of transport of a critically injured trauma patient should **never be delayed** simply to insert IV catheters and administer fluid therapy."

> "Every red blood cell counts." (re: hemorrhage control, Golden Principle 3)

## Evidence-Based Medicine Note

Chapter 2 emphasizes that PHTLS is **knowledge-driven, not purely protocol-driven**. Protocols guide, but the practitioner's understanding of anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology must guide application of those protocols.
