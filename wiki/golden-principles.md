# The 14 Golden Principles of Prehospital Trauma Care

**Summary**: The 14 foundational principles of PHTLS that guide prehospital trauma care — what must be accomplished to maximize the chance of patient survival, regardless of system or setting.

**Sources**: PHTLS Prehospital Trauma Life Support 10th Edition.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## What Are the Golden Principles?

The Golden Principles define **what** the prehospital care practitioner must accomplish to maximize patient survival. They are **principles**, not protocols — they tell you the goal, not the exact method. How each is achieved depends on the practitioner's preferences (see [[principles-vs-preferences]]).

They are complementary to the **XABCDE / MARCH** primary survey and apply to all trauma patients.

---

## The 14 Principles

### 1. Ensure the Safety of the Prehospital Care Practitioners and the Patient

Scene safety is the highest ongoing priority. No patient can be helped by a practitioner who becomes a casualty themselves. Maintaining **situational awareness** throughout the call is essential — conditions can change.

Key chapters: Scene Management (Ch 5), Injury Prevention (Ch 16)

---

### 2. Assess the Scene Situation to Determine the Need for Additional Resources

Before approaching the patient, assess:
- Number of patients
- Mechanism of injury
- Safety hazards
- Resources on scene vs. resources needed

Request additional resources **early** — before it is obviously too late. This principle governs mass-casualty incidents (MCIs) and disaster management.

Key chapters: Scene Management (Ch 5), Disaster Management (Ch 17), WMD (Ch 18)

---

### 3. Control Any Significant External Hemorrhage

**Every red blood cell counts.** Significant external hemorrhage must be controlled immediately — resuscitation will never succeed in the presence of severe ongoing external bleeding.

Methods (in order of escalation):
- Direct pressure
- Wound packing with hemostatic agents (e.g., Combat Gauze)
- Tourniquet (proximal to wound on extremity)

This applies even when blood products are available for prehospital use — hemorrhage control remains paramount.

Key chapters: [[shock-management]], Abdominal Trauma (Ch 11), Musculoskeletal Trauma (Ch 12), Wilderness Care (Ch 21), TEMS (Ch 22)

---

### 4. Use the Primary Survey Approach to Identify Life-Threatening Conditions

The **XABCDE** systematic survey:
- **X** — eXsanguinating hemorrhage (external)
- **A** — Airway
- **B** — Breathing / Ventilation
- **C** — Circulation
- **D** — Disability (neuro)
- **E** — Expose / Environment

"Treat as you go" — as life threats are identified, care is initiated immediately. Many aspects of the survey are performed simultaneously when resources allow.

Key chapter: [[shock-assessment]], Patient Assessment and Management (Ch 6)

---

### 5. Recognize the Physics of Trauma that Produced the Injuries

Approaching the scene, the practitioner should be considering:
- Kinetic energy involved
- Deceleration / acceleration forces
- Penetrating vs. blunt mechanism
- Injury patterns associated with specific mechanisms

Knowledge of injury patterns aids prediction of hidden injuries. It also helps determine destination facility. This should **not delay** patient assessment — it is incorporated into the global scene assessment.

Key chapter: The Physics of Trauma (Ch 4)

---

### 6. Provide Appropriate Airway Management While Maintaining Spinal Motion Restriction as Indicated

After scene safety and hemorrhage control, **airway management is the highest priority**.

Essential skills all practitioners must be able to perform:
- Head and neck stabilization
- Manual clearing of the airway
- Jaw thrust and chin lift
- Suctioning
- Oropharyngeal and nasopharyngeal airways

More advanced interventions (endotracheal intubation, surgical airway) are applied based on practitioner training, local protocols, and patient condition — see [[principles-vs-preferences]].

Key chapters: Airway and Ventilation (Ch 7), Head and Neck Trauma (Ch 8), Spinal Trauma (Ch 9)

---

### 7. Support Ventilation and Deliver Oxygen to Maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94%

Recognize and correct:
- **Bradypnea** (RR too slow) or **tachypnea** (RR too fast)
- Assist ventilation with bag-mask device connected to supplemental oxygen
- All patients with obvious or suspected life-threatening conditions need supplemental oxygen

Target: **SpO₂ ≥ 94%**

Key chapter: Airway and Ventilation (Ch 7), Head and Neck Trauma (Ch 8)

---

### 8. Provide Basic Shock Therapy, Including Appropriately Splinting Musculoskeletal Injuries and Restoring and Maintaining Normal Body Temperature

Once external hemorrhage is controlled:
- **Splinting fractures** — a fracture produces internal bleeding that cannot be visually identified or stopped by bandaging; realigning the limb may reduce blood loss
- **Normothermia** — hypothermia drastically impairs blood clotting. Use blankets and a warmed ambulance interior

> "If the practitioner is comfortable in the patient compartment, it is too cold for the patient." ([[lethal-triad]])

Key chapters: [[shock-management]], Musculoskeletal Trauma (Ch 12), Environmental Trauma (Ch 19), Wilderness Care (Ch 21)

---

### 9. Apply Appropriate Spinal Motion Restriction Principles Based on the Patient's Complaints and Mental Status and Considering the Mechanism of Injury

Manual stabilization and motion restriction of the cervical and thoracolumbar spine should be established and maintained throughout transport **or until the patient is deemed not to meet indications**.

Spinal motion restriction is **indication-based**, not applied to all trauma patients.

Key chapter: Spinal Trauma (Ch 9), Wilderness Care (Ch 21)

---

### 10. For Critically Injured Trauma Patients, Initiate Transport to the Closest Appropriate Facility as Soon as Possible After EMS Arrival on Scene

**"Platinum 10 Minutes"** — critically injured patients should be transported within 10 minutes of EMS arrival on scene wherever possible.

- The closest hospital may **not** be the most appropriate facility
- Consider: surgical capabilities, trauma center level (see [[trauma-centers]]), blood bank, transport time

This principle exists because most critically injured trauma patients are in hemorrhagic [[shock]] and need two things unavailable in the field: blood and plasma.

Key chapters: [[golden-period]], Patient Assessment and Management (Ch 6), Head and Neck Trauma (Ch 8), Thoracic Trauma (Ch 10), Burns (Ch 13)

---

### 11. Initiate Fluid Replacement En Route to the Receiving Facility as Necessary to Restore Basic Perfusion

**Transport is never delayed to insert IV catheters.** Fluid replacement is initiated **en route**.

Crystalloid solutions restore volume and improve perfusion but **do not transport oxygen**. Blood and plasma remain the preferred resuscitation fluids for trauma patients when available.

Balanced resuscitation (permissive hypotension for penetrating trauma) — see [[shock-management]].

Key chapters: [[shock-management]], Burns (Ch 13)

---

### 12. Ascertain the Patient's Medical History, and Perform a Secondary Survey When Life-Threatening Problems Have Been Satisfactorily Managed or Ruled Out

The secondary survey is comprehensive head-to-toe assessment conducted **after** life threats are controlled or ruled out. It includes:
- SAMPLE history (Signs/Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past medical history, Last oral intake, Events)
- Full physical examination
- Pain assessment

Key chapter: Patient Assessment and Management (Ch 6)

---

### 13. Provide Adequate Pain Relief

Pain management is a component of trauma care that must not be overlooked. While life-threatening conditions take absolute priority, pain relief should be provided as soon as it is safe to do so.

Pain management consideration spans all patient populations and injury types (burns, fractures, thoracic trauma, pediatric, geriatric).

Key chapters: Patient Assessment and Management (Ch 6), Thoracic Trauma (Ch 10), Burns (Ch 13), Musculoskeletal Trauma (Ch 12)

---

### 14. Provide Thorough and Accurate Communication Regarding the Patient and the Circumstances of the Injury to the Receiving Facility

Communication about a trauma patient involves three components:
1. **Prearrival warning** — advance radio/phone report to receiving facility
2. **Verbal report upon arrival** — concise handoff to receiving team
3. **Written documentation** — patient care report (PCR)

Trauma care is a team effort. The prehospital team's report determines how prepared the receiving team is. Poor communication wastes time and risks patient safety.

Key chapter: Patient Assessment and Management (Ch 6)
